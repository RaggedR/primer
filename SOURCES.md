# The Primer — Primary Source Curriculum

> 50–100 primary historical sources for bright 14–18 year olds. Individual mentors select 5–6 to work through deeply with their student. The goal is not coverage — it's the skill of reading a primary source, holding contradictions, and building your own understanding.

## How to Use This List

Each source has:
- **What it is** — the document, who wrote it, when
- **Why it's here** — what it teaches about reading the past
- **Pairs with** — sources that contradict, complicate, or extend it
- **Difficulty** — accessible (A), moderate (M), challenging (C)

A Miranda should pick sources that create *tension* — not a coherent narrative, but an argument the student must resolve for themselves.

## I. Power and Its Justifications

*How do people explain why they should rule others?*

1. **Code of Hammurabi** (~1754 BCE) — excerpts on property, injury, class. The first surviving legal code. Note how punishments differ by social class — the law isn't blind, and wasn't pretending to be.
   - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 2, 5

2. **Pericles' Funeral Oration** (431 BCE, recorded by Thucydides) — Athens' self-image at the height of its power. Democracy, freedom, glory. Ask: who is not in the room? (Women, slaves, non-citizens — the majority.)
   - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 3, 17

3. **The Melian Dialogue** (416 BCE, Thucydides) — Athens tells the small island of Melos: submit or die. "The strong do what they can, the weak suffer what they must." Same Athens as the Funeral Oration, five years later.
   - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 2

4. **Asoka's Rock Edicts** (~260 BCE) — An emperor who conquered violently, then renounced violence and carved his remorse into stone across India. Power choosing restraint. How much do you trust it?
   - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 1, 5

5. **Mandate of Heaven — from the Shujing** (compiled ~4th c. BCE) — The Chinese justification for revolution: heaven withdraws its mandate from unjust rulers. The ruled have the right to rebel. Compare with European divine right.
   - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 6, 8

6. **James I, "The Trew Law of Free Monarchies"** (1598) — A king explains why kings answer to God alone, not to their people. Sincere, articulate, and chilling.
   - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 5, 8, 17

7. **Bartolomé de las Casas, "A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies"** (1542) — A Spanish priest documents atrocities against Indigenous peoples. One of the first human rights documents. Written to persuade a king, not a public.
   - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 8, 12

8. **Declaration of Independence** (1776) — "All men are created equal," written by a slaveholder. The most important contradiction in modern political history. Read it alongside Frederick Douglass (#17).
   - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 5, 6, 17

9. **Napoleon's Proclamation to the Egyptians** (1798) — A European general tells a colonised people he has come to liberate them. The template for every military "liberation" since.
   - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 7, 12

10. **Leopold II's letters on the Congo** (1880s–1900s) — Private correspondence revealing the calculated exploitation behind the public philanthropy. The gap between what power says and what power does.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 7, 9

## II. Resistance and Revolution

*What does it sound like when people refuse?*

11. **Spartacus — Plutarch's and Appian's accounts** (~1st c. BCE/CE) — Two Roman historians describe the slave revolt. Neither is sympathetic. What can you learn about the rebels from sources written by their enemies?
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 17, 12

12. **Tupac Amaru II's proclamation** (1780) — An Indigenous Peruvian leader's declaration of rebellion against Spanish rule. Written in legal language, claiming rights within the colonial system while overturning it.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 7, 8

13. **Declaration of the Rights of Woman and the Female Citizen — Olympe de Gouges** (1791) — A direct rewrite of the Declaration of the Rights of Man with "woman" substituted. She was guillotined two years later.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 8, 14

14. **Sojourner Truth, "Ain't I a Woman?"** (1851) — A formerly enslaved woman dismantles the intersection of racial and gender oppression in a few hundred words. (Note: the famous version may be embellished by the transcriber — that's itself a lesson in sources.)
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 13, 17

15. **The Communist Manifesto — opening and closing sections** (1848) — Not as scripture, but as a primary source. What did Marx and Engels see in 1848 that made this feel urgent? What did they get right about industrial capitalism? What did they miss?
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 16, 20

16. **A mill girl's letters from Lowell, Massachusetts** (1840s) — Factory workers describing their own conditions. Compare with what the factory owners said. Compare with Marx.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 15, 20

17. **Frederick Douglass, "What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?"** (1852) — The greatest American speech. He stands before an audience celebrating freedom and asks them to hear what freedom sounds like from the other side.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 8, 2

18. **Gandhi's letter to Hitler** (1939) — Brief, earnest, naive, brave. Gandhi applies his philosophy of non-violence to the most extreme test case. Does it hold? A letter that raises more questions than it answers.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 25, 4

19. **Nelson Mandela's speech from the dock** (1964) — "An ideal for which I am prepared to die." A man facing execution explains himself to the court that will condemn him.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 17, 12

20. **Lech Wałęsa's 21 Demands** (1980) — A shipyard electrician's list of demands that cracked the Soviet bloc. Concrete, practical, revolutionary. Workers' rights as world-historical force.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 15, 16

## III. Science and the Overthrow of Certainty

*What happens when someone sees what wasn't supposed to be there?*

21. **Galileo's letter to the Grand Duchess Christina** (1615) — A scientist explains to power why scripture and nature cannot contradict each other. The argument for intellectual freedom, written carefully enough to not get killed (it almost didn't work).
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 22, 6

22. **Darwin's letter to Asa Gray** (1860) — "I cannot persuade myself that a beneficent and omnipotent God would have designedly created the Ichneumonidae with the express intention of their feeding within the living bodies of Caterpillars." Private doubt from a man who knew what his theory meant.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 21, 23

23. **Einstein's letter to Roosevelt** (1939) — A physicist tells a president that atoms can be split into bombs. The moment science became geopolitics. Two pages that changed everything.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 25, 28

24. **Rosalind Franklin's lab notebooks** (1952) — The X-ray crystallography data that revealed DNA's structure. She did the work. Watson and Crick got the credit. The notebooks are meticulous, brilliant, and heartbreaking in context.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 13, 14

25. **Oppenheimer's "Now I am become Death" interview** (1965) — A scientist reckoning with what he built. Compare with his earlier enthusiasm. When does knowledge become guilt?
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 23, 28

26. **Rachel Carson, excerpts from "Silent Spring"** (1962) — A scientist writing for the public about pesticides and ecological collapse. She was attacked as hysterical. She was right. The template for every environmental warning since.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 22

27. **Euler, selected letters from "Letters to a German Princess"** (1768) — The greatest mathematician of his age, teaching physics to a teenage girl with complete respect for her intelligence. The model for what education can be.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 24, 21

28. **Szilard's petition to the President** (1945) — Leo Szilard, who helped build the bomb, petitions Truman not to use it on civilians. Signed by 70 scientists. It was classified and never reached Truman in time.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 23, 25

## IV. War and Its Witnesses

*What does it look like from inside?*

29. **Thucydides on the Plague of Athens** (430 BCE) — Civilisation dissolving under pressure. Laws abandoned, religion meaningless, the sick left to die. Written by someone who caught it and survived.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 2, 3

30. **A Crusader's account and an Arab account of the Fall of Jerusalem** (1099) — The same day, two perspectives. The Crusader thanks God. The Arab records the massacre. Neither is lying.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 33

31. **Aztec accounts of the Spanish conquest** (compiled 1550s, from the Florentine Codex) — The conquered describing their own destruction. Recorded in Nahuatl by Sahagún's informants. What does defeat sound like in the voice of the defeated?
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 7, 9

32. **Wilfred Owen, selected poems** (1917–18) — "Dulce et Decorum Est," "Anthem for Doomed Youth." A soldier-poet who dismantled the glory of war from the trenches. He was killed one week before the Armistice.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 33, 34

33. **Ernst Jünger, excerpts from "Storm of Steel"** (1920) — A German soldier who found beauty and meaning in the same war that destroyed Owen. Same trenches, opposite conclusions. Neither is wrong about what they experienced.
    - Difficulty: C | Pairs with: 32

34. **Primo Levi, excerpts from "If This Is a Man"** (1947) — A chemist's account of Auschwitz. Clinical, precise, devastating. The most important testimony of the 20th century.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 35, 36

35. **Hannah Arendt, excerpts from "Eichmann in Jerusalem"** (1963) — "The banality of evil." A Jewish philosopher watches a Nazi bureaucrat on trial and sees not a monster but a clerk. Controversial when published, essential now.
    - Difficulty: C | Pairs with: 34, 10

36. **Hiroshima survivor testimonies (hibakusha)** — Selected first-person accounts. The other side of Einstein's letter and Oppenheimer's regret.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 23, 25, 28

37. **A North Vietnamese soldier's diary** (1960s) — Personal entries from someone fighting on the other side of America's war. Homesick, afraid, idealistic. The enemy as a person.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 38

38. **Tim O'Brien, "How to Tell a True War Story" from The Things They Carried** (1990) — A Vietnam veteran on the impossibility of telling the truth about war through narrative. Meta-primary source: a source about sources.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 37, 32

## V. Daily Life and Hidden Voices

*The people history usually ignores.*

39. **Letters of Pliny the Younger** — selected (1st–2nd c. CE) — A Roman aristocrat's casual correspondence: dinner parties, legal cases, the eruption of Vesuvius. Daily life in an empire, from someone who thought it would last forever.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 29

40. **Sei Shōnagon, excerpts from "The Pillow Book"** (~1000 CE) — A Japanese court lady's observations: things that are hateful, things that make your heart beat fast, things that are elegant. A thousand-year-old blog post. Proof that people have always been people.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 41

41. **Geniza documents from medieval Cairo** (10th–13th c.) — Fragments of everyday Jewish life in the Islamic world: business letters, marriage contracts, shopping lists, legal disputes. The most intimate primary sources surviving from the medieval period. Found in a synagogue storeroom because Jewish law prohibits destroying texts containing God's name — so everything was saved.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 40, 30

42. **Olaudah Equiano, excerpts from "The Interesting Narrative"** (1789) — A formerly enslaved man's autobiography. The Middle Passage described by someone who survived it. Published to support abolition — ask how that purpose shapes the telling.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 17, 7

43. **Diary of a young woman during the Irish Famine** (1840s) — Asenath Nicholson or similar account. Hunger described from inside. Compare with British parliamentary debates about whether to send food.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 16, 10

44. **Letters from Chinese railroad workers in America** (1860s–1870s) — Fragments, mostly translated. The people who built the infrastructure of a country that excluded them.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 16, 44

45. **Diary of a Soviet citizen under Stalin** — selections from "Intimacy and Terror" or similar collections. What does daily life look like when you cannot write honestly even in your own diary?
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 10, 34

46. **Hana Brady's suitcase / children's artifacts from the Holocaust** — Objects and documents from children. The youngest, most voiceless victims. Sometimes an artifact is more powerful than a text.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 34

47. **Accounts from the Partition of India** (1947) — Oral histories from both sides of the border. Neighbours became enemies overnight. The human cost of drawing lines on maps.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 30, 43

## VI. Ideas That Changed the Frame

*Moments when someone said something that rearranged how people thought.*

48. **Plato, the Allegory of the Cave** (from "The Republic," ~375 BCE) — The foundational metaphor for education: most people see shadows and mistake them for reality. The philosopher's job is to turn around. What does the light feel like?
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 21, 56

49. **Maimonides, excerpts from "Guide for the Perplexed"** (~1190) — A Jewish philosopher in Islamic Spain reconciling reason and revelation. The argument that faith and intellect are not enemies. Written as a letter to a confused student.
    - Difficulty: C | Pairs with: 21, 27, 52

50. **Ibn Khaldun, excerpts from "The Muqaddimah"** (1377) — An Arab historian invents sociology. Civilisations rise and fall in cycles; group solidarity (asabiyyah) determines which tribes conquer and which decay. Written 400 years before European sociology existed.
    - Difficulty: C | Pairs with: 5, 29

51. **Martin Luther, the 95 Theses** (1517) — A monk's complaint list nailed to a church door. It split Christianity in half. The original viral document.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 6, 21

52. **Spinoza, excerpts from the "Theological-Political Treatise"** (1670) — A Jewish lens-grinder, excommunicated by his own community, argues for freedom of thought and the separation of religion and state. He had to publish anonymously. The foundations of secular liberalism from an exile.
    - Difficulty: C | Pairs with: 49, 21

53. **Mary Wollstonecraft, excerpts from "A Vindication of the Rights of Woman"** (1792) — The first systematic argument for women's education and equality. Written in fury, published to outrage. She was called a "hyena in petticoats."
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 13, 14

54. **W.E.B. Du Bois, "Of Our Spiritual Strivings" from The Souls of Black Folk** (1903) — "How does it feel to be a problem?" Double consciousness — seeing yourself through the eyes of those who despise you. The most penetrating description of what racism does to the mind.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 17, 35

55. **Simone Weil, "The Iliad, or the Poem of Force"** (1939) — A French philosopher reads Homer and finds a universal statement about what violence does to everyone it touches — victors and victims alike. Written as Europe was about to prove her right.
    - Difficulty: C | Pairs with: 32, 33, 34

56. **Václav Havel, "The Power of the Powerless"** (1978) — A playwright explains how totalitarian systems survive through ordinary people's small compliances, and how refusing to "live within the lie" is itself revolutionary. The greengrocer who stops putting the sign in his window.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 20, 45

## VII. The View from Here

*Sources for understanding the present as history.*

57. **Alan Turing, "Computing Machinery and Intelligence"** (1950) — "Can machines think?" The paper that launched AI as a question. Written by a man who would be chemically castrated by his government three years later for being gay. The personal and the intellectual cannot be separated.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 27, 24

58. **Rachel Corrie's emails from Gaza** (2003) — A 23-year-old American activist writing to her parents from a war zone. She was killed by a bulldozer weeks later. Raw, young, unfinished — a primary source from a conflict that hasn't ended.
    - Difficulty: M | Pairs with: 37, 36

59. **Edward Snowden's open letter** (2013) — A systems administrator explains why he leaked classified surveillance programs. Treason or conscience? The source doesn't answer — the reader must.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 56, 23

60. **Malala Yousafzai's BBC diary** (2009) — An 11-year-old Pakistani girl blogs about life under the Taliban. Written before she was shot, before she was famous. The unfiltered voice.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 53, 19

61. **Greta Thunberg's speech to the UN** (2019) — "How dare you." A teenager addresses world leaders. Compare with Douglass (#17) — the structural similarity is exact. The powerless standing before the powerful, using their own stated values against them.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 17, 26

62. **Selected tweets and posts from the Arab Spring** (2011) — Real-time primary sources from a revolution. The first uprising that was documented by its participants as it happened. Some of those people are now dead or in prison.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 20, 56

63. **"Woman, Life, Freedom" — documents from the Mahsa Amini protests** (2022–) — Statements, social media posts, and testimony from the Iranian women's movement. History that hasn't finished happening.
    - Difficulty: A | Pairs with: 13, 14, 53, 60

## VIII. The Talmudic Pairs

*These are not individual sources but structured confrontations. A Miranda picks one pair and works through both sides.*

- **Pericles (#2) vs the Melian Dialogue (#3)** — Democracy's self-image vs democracy's imperialism
- **Declaration of Independence (#8) vs Douglass (#17)** — Freedom declared vs freedom denied
- **Owen (#32) vs Jünger (#33)** — The same war, opposite meanings
- **Einstein's letter (#23) vs hibakusha testimony (#36)** — The physics and the flesh
- **Gandhi to Hitler (#18) vs Mandela from the dock (#19)** — Non-violence's limits
- **De las Casas (#7) vs Aztec accounts (#31)** — Two critiques of conquest, neither from the conquerors
- **Crusader vs Arab accounts of Jerusalem (#30)** — Same day, two worlds
- **Marx (#15) vs the mill girls (#16)** — Theory of exploitation vs experience of exploitation
- **Galileo (#21) vs Maimonides (#49)** — Reason and faith, two strategies for coexistence
- **Pliny (#39) vs Sei Shōnagon (#40)** — Rome and Kyoto, a millennium apart, the same human impulse to notice and record

---

## Notes for Mirandas

- **Never assign more than 6 sources in a sequence.** Depth over breadth. A student who has truly grappled with three primary sources has learned more than one who has skimmed thirty.
- **Always include at least one pair that creates genuine tension.** The point is not to confirm what the student already believes.
- **Let the student struggle.** Do not pre-interpret. Ask questions: *Who wrote this? Why? What can't they see? What would someone on the other side say?*
- **The difficulty ratings are guidelines, not gates.** A motivated 14-year-old can handle a "C" source with the right scaffolding. A bored 18-year-old will bounce off an "A."
- **The Talmudic method applies to every source:** state what you think it means, find the objection, attempt resolution, identify what changes in practice. Preserve the disagreement where it's real.
