ACCOUNTS FROM THE PARTITION OF INDIA (1947) -- Oral Histories

The Partition of British India on August 15, 1947 created the independent
nations of India and Pakistan. It triggered one of the largest mass
migrations in human history: more than 14 million people were displaced,
and an estimated 1-2 million were killed in communal violence between
Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs.

The oral histories below are drawn from several archives that have worked
to preserve the voices of Partition survivors before they are lost.

==========================================================================

TESTIMONY 1: NASEEM SANI

Source: Citizens' Archive of Pakistan Oral History Project,
as quoted in TIME Magazine, August 2018.

"We were going in a tonga [rickshaw] and I remember there was a truck full
of dead bodies passing... I think they had collected all the Hindus that
were killed. I still remember the arms and legs, you know... when I saw
that I got such a scare."

==========================================================================

TESTIMONY 2: SURVIVOR ON AN ATTACKED TRAIN

Source: 1947 Partition Archive, as reported by NPR, August 2017.

A woman's grandmother, who fled from Peshawar to India, survived a
harrowing journey on a train that was attacked by a mob. She "just sat
quietly in a corner pretending she'd been killed too, so she wouldn't
be noticed."

The phenomenon of "blood trains" -- trains arriving at their destinations
carrying only corpses -- became one of the most harrowing symbols of
Partition violence. Railway station workers described opening train doors
"caked shut with blood," coaches locked tight with bodies visible through
the windows, and survivors emerging in silence.

==========================================================================

TESTIMONY 3: ALI SHAN (San Francisco Bay Area)

Source: 1947 Partition Archive oral history interview,
as reported by the National Endowment for the Humanities.

Ali Shan was eight years old when a mob murdered his family in 1947. He
became the sole survivor and lived alone in refugee camps. During his
oral history interview -- decades later -- he spoke of forgiving his
family's murderers for the first time.

==========================================================================

TESTIMONY 4: HARDEEP SINGH (California, triple refugee)

Source: 1947 Partition Archive, as reported by NEH.

Hardeep Singh's family was displaced three times: from Burma during the
Japanese bombing in 1941, then again during the Partition in 1947. He
recalls:

"I was just a month, but I remember details vividly.... Many perished
along the way."

Reflecting on whether such violence could happen again:

"It can happen here also."

==========================================================================

TESTIMONY 5: ARGHWANI BEGUM

Source: Ritam Digital archive of Partition testimony.

Arghwani Begum's train was attacked at the Wagah border crossing. Amid
the panic, her infant son struggled to breathe until a man on the train
helped the baby get air through the main door. She recalls seeing
countless bodies of men, women, and children that night.

==========================================================================

TESTIMONY 6: SUDERSHANA KUMARI

Source: 1947 Partition Archive.

Sudershana Kumari was eight years old at the time of Partition. She
remembers how her family had to suddenly leave their home one evening
in the middle of preparing dinner -- abandoning everything they owned
and fleeing with only what they could carry.

==========================================================================

TESTIMONY 7: FROM THE SOAS ORAL HISTORY COLLECTION

Source: "India: A People Partitioned" -- BBC World Service radio series
(1997; 2000). Collection held at the School of Oriental and African
Studies (SOAS), University of London. 205 interviews compiled by
broadcaster and historian Andrew Whitehead.

The interviews focus on the lived experience of Partition among ordinary
people of Hindu, Muslim, and Sikh heritage, covering key events including:
- Independence celebrations turning to fear
- Communal violence between former neighbors
- The mass movement of refugee columns
- Women's experiences, including abduction and "recovery"
- Life in refugee camps
- The experience of arriving in a new country with nothing

==========================================================================

TESTIMONY 8: ANCHAL MALHOTRA (oral historian, grandson of survivors)

Source: Global Voices, August 2019.

Malhotra, who has collected Partition stories through material objects,
recalls his grandmother's words:

"Ask me quickly before I forget. Otherwise it would die with me."

On the experience of displacement:

"Many people didn't quite understand what was happening when the
partition was happening.... An entire population can be forced to
migrate to a place where they don't want without quite understanding
what was happening."

"People always say in interviews that there was a madness in the end.
Nobody understood what was happening."

==========================================================================

CONTEXT NOTE:

The most quoted oral survivor:

"The only solace we had was that we were in this together."

This line, repeated across multiple survivor accounts, captures the
paradox of Partition: a catastrophe so vast that shared suffering became
the only form of consolation.

==========================================================================

NOTE FOR EDUCATORS:

The Partition of India is one of the most significant and least-taught
events of the 20th century. Oral histories are the primary vehicle for
this history because:

1. Official records focus on political negotiations, not human experience
2. The violence was too chaotic and widespread for systematic documentation
3. Survivors were often too traumatized to speak for decades
4. The event is politically sensitive in both India and Pakistan
5. Many survivors are now in their 80s and 90s -- their stories will soon
   be lost forever

The 1947 Partition Archive has recorded over 12,000 interviews in 22
languages from 14 countries. A portion is available through Stanford
University Libraries.
