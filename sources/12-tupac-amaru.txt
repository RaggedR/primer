TUPAC AMARU II'S PROCLAMATION (1780)
An Indigenous Peruvian leader's declaration of rebellion against Spanish rule.

NOTE ON THIS SOURCE: The full text of Tupac Amaru II's proclamations in English
translation is difficult to obtain outside specialized academic anthologies. The
most complete collection is in Ward Stavig and Ella Schmidt, eds., "The Tupac Amaru
and Catarista Rebellions: An Anthology of Sources" (Hackett, 2008). What follows
are the most substantial excerpts available from multiple sources. For classroom
use, the Stavig/Schmidt anthology is the recommended source for complete texts.

================================================================================

CONTEXT

Jose Gabriel Condorcanqui, who took the name Tupac Amaru II (claiming descent
from the last Inca ruler Tupac Amaru, executed by the Spanish in 1572), led the
largest indigenous rebellion in the history of Spain's American colonies. He was
a curaca (indigenous leader) who had been educated in Spanish institutions and
initially sought reform through legal channels before turning to armed revolt.

The rebellion began on November 4, 1780, with the capture and execution of the
corregidor (colonial administrator) Antonio de Arriaga in Tinta, near Cusco.

================================================================================

PROCLAMATION EXCERPTS

From his early proclamations, Tupac Amaru declared:

"That there have been repeated outcries directed to me by the indigenous peoples
of this and surrounding provinces, outcries against the abuses committed by
European-born crown officials... Justified outcries that have produced no remedy
from the royal courts."

"I have acted... only against the mentioned abuses and to preserve the peace and
well-being of Indians, mestizos, zambos, as well as native-born whites and blacks."

In styling himself as ruler, he proclaimed:

"I, Don Jose I, by the grace of God, Inca, King of Peru, Santa Fe [Bogota],
Quito, Chile, Buenos Aires, and the continents of the South Seas, Duke of the
Superlative, Lord of the Caesars and Amazons with dominion over the great Paititi,
Commissar distributor of the divine mercy, etc."

================================================================================

THE ABOLITION OF SLAVERY DECREE (November 16, 1780)

On November 16, 1780, Tupac Amaru issued a proclamation decreeing the emancipation
of Afro-Peruvian and African slaves -- the first such decree in the history of
Spanish America. In this document, he exhorted all "decent" Spaniards, the clergy,
and others friendly with the Peruvian population to join the fight against the
hostilities and abuses of the European population; and all those who had been
mistreated by the "chapetones" (a derogatory term for peninsular Spaniards),
including slaves, to abandon them.

This decision served a tactical purpose: with the escape of the slaves, the
colonial export economy would collapse and the rebel forces would grow. The
psychological effect was also calculated: in Lima, the upper classes on their
haciendas would be more concerned about defiant slaves and free blacks and
mulattos than about the population of the highlands.

================================================================================

PROCLAMATION TO THE PEOPLE OF LAMPA (November 25, 1780)

On November 25, 1780, Tupac Amaru wrote a proclamation addressed to the population
of Lampa, announcing his campaign against Spanish tyranny and abuses, as well as
his commitment to the Creoles. At the same time, he boasted of having 6,000
followers, including indigenous people, Creoles, and people from outside the area.

His demands included:
- Ending the mita (rotational forced labor system)
- Limiting the power of the corregidores
- The emancipation of slaves
- The creation of a new audiencia (court) at Cusco
- Freedom for the indigenous population

================================================================================

AFTERMATH

Tupac Amaru was captured in April 1781 and executed on May 18, 1781 in the main
plaza of Cusco. The colonial authorities forced him to watch the execution of his
wife Micaela Bastidas, his sons, and other family members before his own death.

In retaliation, the Spanish colonial authorities banned the Quechua language, the
wearing of indigenous clothing, and virtually any mention or commemoration of Inca
culture and history. Yet the rebellion led directly to the abolition of the
corregidor system and other colonial reforms.

Tupac Amaru's final words reportedly were: "I will return, and I will be millions."
