ROSALIND FRANKLIN
Selected Notebook Entries and Research Notes on DNA Structure (1951-1953)
======================================================================

NOTE ON THIS SOURCE: Rosalind Franklin's original lab notebooks are
held in archives at Churchill College, Cambridge, and King's College
London. The Wellcome Collection holds annotated copies as part of the
"Codebreakers: Makers of Modern Genetics" collection. The National
Library of Medicine (profiles.nlm.nih.gov/spotlight/kr) has digitized
selections of her papers. Full transcriptions of the notebooks have
not been published as standalone texts. What follows is a compilation
of Franklin's own words as quoted in scholarly sources, reconstructing
her progressive understanding of DNA's structure.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
SEMINAR NOTES, NOVEMBER 1951
----------------------------------------------------------------------

In November 1951, Franklin presented her X-ray diffraction findings
at a seminar at King's College London (attended by James Watson). Her
notes described DNA as:

"a big helix or a smaller helix consisting of several chains"

She further noted:

"The phosphates are on the outside so that phosphate-phosphate
inter-helical bonds are disrupted by water."

Her notes were detailed, describing two forms of DNA (the A and B
forms) and laying out their differences, including how the structure
switches from A to B depending on the relative humidity in the sample
chamber.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PHOTOGRAPH 51, MAY 1952
----------------------------------------------------------------------

In May 1952, Franklin and her graduate student Raymond Gosling
produced the famous X-ray diffraction photograph known as "Photo 51"
-- the clearest image yet obtained of the B form of DNA. The
characteristic X-shaped pattern in the photograph was strong evidence
for a helical structure. Franklin's careful experimental technique
produced what would become one of the most important photographs in
the history of science.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
MRC REPORT, DECEMBER 1952
----------------------------------------------------------------------

In her section of the 1952 King's College Medical Research Council
(MRC) report, Franklin provided quantitative measurements for the
interphosphate distances and confirmed the external placement of the
phosphate groups. She reported that the sugar-phosphate backbone forms
the outside of the helix and that each chain repeats itself after one
revolution in 34 Angstroms.

This report was circulated internally and was seen by Max Perutz, who
shared it with Watson and Crick -- providing them with crucial
empirical measurements for their model-building.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
NOTEBOOK ENTRY, FEBRUARY 1953
----------------------------------------------------------------------

By late February 1953, Franklin's notes reveal that she had accepted
that DNA had a helical structure, probably with two strands. She wrote:

"Evidence for 2-chain (or 1-chain helix)"

Her notes show she had also recognized that the component nucleotides
on each strand were related in a way that made the strands
complementary, though she had not yet determined that the two strands
run in opposite directions (antiparallel).

----------------------------------------------------------------------
DRAFT MANUSCRIPT, MARCH 1953
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Franklin was preparing a manuscript on the B form of DNA when Watson
and Crick's model was announced. She had independently concluded that
DNA was a double helix with the phosphates on the outside.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PUBLISHED PAPER: "Molecular Configuration in Sodium Thymonucleate"
Nature, Vol. 171, pp. 740-741, April 25, 1953
(Published alongside Watson & Crick's paper)
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Franklin and Gosling's paper appeared in the same issue of Nature as
Watson and Crick's famous paper. Their paper presented X-ray
diffraction data supporting the double helix model. They wrote:

"Thus our general ideas are not inconsistent with the model proposed
by Watson and Crick in the preceding communication."

----------------------------------------------------------------------
CONTEXT
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Franklin's meticulous experimental work -- particularly Photo 51 and
her MRC report data -- provided essential evidence for the double
helix model. Watson and Crick saw Photo 51 without Franklin's
knowledge (shown to Watson by Maurice Wilkins in January 1953) and
received her MRC report data through Max Perutz. Franklin was not
informed that her data had been shared.

Franklin died of ovarian cancer on April 16, 1958, at the age of 37,
likely caused by her extensive exposure to X-ray radiation during her
research. Watson, Crick, and Wilkins received the Nobel Prize in
Physiology or Medicine in 1962. Nobel Prizes are not awarded
posthumously.

The notebooks remain a testament to her extraordinary experimental
skill and the intellectual independence with which she approached one
of the greatest scientific puzzles of the twentieth century.
