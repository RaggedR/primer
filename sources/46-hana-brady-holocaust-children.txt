HANA BRADY'S SUITCASE AND CHILDREN'S ARTIFACTS FROM THE HOLOCAUST

Hana Brady (1931-1944) was a Czech Jewish girl from Nove Mesto na Morave,
Czechoslovakia, who was murdered in the gas chambers of Auschwitz on
October 23, 1944, at age thirteen.

Her story became known through a suitcase she carried to the Theresienstadt
(Terezin) concentration camp, which bore her name, birthdate (May 16, 1931),
and the German word "Waisenkind" (orphan) written in large letters. The
suitcase was preserved at the Auschwitz Museum and later became the subject
of Karen Levine's book "Hana's Suitcase" (2002) after Japanese educator
Fumiko Ishioka traced its history for the Tokyo Holocaust Education Resource
Center.

Hana's brother George Brady survived the Holocaust and later settled in
Toronto, Canada, where he spent decades sharing his and Hana's story with
schoolchildren.

HANA'S DRAWINGS FROM THERESIENSTADT

During her imprisonment at Theresienstadt (1942-1944), Hana created at
least five paintings, which she signed with her name and the date. These
drawings -- two in color and three in black and white -- survived the war
and were later found among the 4,500 children's artworks preserved by
the Jewish Museum in Prague.

One drawing, made in 1944, shows a tree. Another depicts people carrying
luggage and boarding a train -- an image that takes on devastating meaning
knowing that Hana was herself transported by train to Auschwitz.

These drawings were created in secret art classes organized by Friedl
Dicker-Brandeis (1898-1944), an Austrian artist and Bauhaus student who
was also imprisoned at Theresienstadt. Dicker-Brandeis used art therapy
with the children, encouraging them to draw their memories, dreams, and
daily experiences. Before her own deportation to Auschwitz, she hid two
suitcases containing 4,500 of the children's drawings. She was murdered
on October 9, 1944 -- two weeks before Hana.

CHILDREN'S POETRY FROM THERESIENSTADT (TEREZIN)

Of the approximately 15,000 children who passed through Theresienstadt
between 1942 and 1944, only 132 survived.

Though education was officially forbidden, teachers held secret classes
in the children's dormitories. Poetry contests and recitation evenings
were organized. The following poems were preserved and later published
in "I Never Saw Another Butterfly: Children's Drawings and Poems from
Terezin Concentration Camp, 1942-1944" (compiled by Hana Volavkova,
first published in Czech in 1959, English edition 1964).


THE BUTTERFLY
by Pavel Friedmann (1921-1944)
Written June 4, 1942, at Theresienstadt
Pavel was murdered at Auschwitz on September 29, 1944.

The last, the very last,
So richly, brightly, dazzlingly yellow.
Perhaps if the sun's tears would sing
against a white stone...

Such, such a yellow
Is carried lightly 'way up high.
It went away I'm sure because it wished to
kiss the world goodbye.

For seven weeks I've lived in here,
Penned up inside this ghetto
But I have found my people here.
The dandelions call to me
And the white chestnut candles in the court.
Only I never saw another butterfly.

That butterfly was the last one.
Butterflies don't live in here,
In the ghetto.

ADDITIONAL CHILDREN'S ARTIFACTS

The Theresienstadt collection includes not only drawings and poems but also:

- Magazines secretly produced by children in the dormitories, including
  "Vedem" (meaning "We Lead"), a weekly magazine produced by boys in
  Dormitory 1 from 1942-1944, containing poetry, stories, essays, reviews,
  and drawings. Over 800 pages survive.

- Musical compositions, including the children's opera "Brundibar" by
  Hans Krasa, performed 55 times at Theresienstadt before the cast and
  composer were deported to Auschwitz.

- Letters written by children to family members who had already been
  deported, which were never sent.

- Diaries kept in secret, most of which were destroyed or lost.

NOTE FOR EDUCATORS:

Hana's suitcase works as a primary source because it is simultaneously an
ordinary object (every child has packed a bag) and an artifact of genocide.
The suitcase makes the abstract concrete: one name, one birthdate, one word
("orphan"), one life.

The children's art and poetry from Theresienstadt are primary sources that
demonstrate both the horror of the Holocaust and the resilience of the human
spirit. They are age-appropriate entry points because they were created by
children.
