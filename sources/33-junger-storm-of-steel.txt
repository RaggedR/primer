ERNST JUNGER, EXCERPTS FROM "STORM OF STEEL" (1920)
(In Stahlgewittern)
Original German publication 1920; English translation by
Basil Creighton 1929. Junger revised the book multiple times.

NOTE: "Storm of Steel" remains under copyright. The excerpts below
are drawn from publicly available quotations, academic sources, and
the 1929 Creighton translation available on Internet Archive. They
are presented here for educational use under fair use/fair dealing.

---

From the opening:

We had come from lecture halls, school desks and factory workbenches,
and over the brief weeks of training, we had bonded together into one
large and enthusiastic group. Grown up in an age of security, we
shared a yearning for danger, for the experience of the extraordinary.
We were enraptured by war.

---

On first arriving at the front:

The moment I heard the sharp hiss of the first bullets near me and
the thunderclap of bursting shells, I realized that I was in the
presence of an utterly unfamiliar power. For the first time I had the
sense of being in the grip of something from which there was no escape.

---

On trench warfare:

Trench fighting is the bloodiest, wildest, most brutal of all...
Of all the war's exciting moments, none is so powerful as the meeting
of two storm troop leaders between narrow trench walls. There's no
retreat and no mercy then. Blood sounds in the shrill cry that is
wrung like a nightmare from the breast.

---

On a night attack:

A bloody scene with no witnesses was about to happen. It was the
home of the great god Pain, and for the first time I looked through
a devilish chink into the mysteries of the underworld.

---

On the Battle of the Somme:

The sunken road now appeared as nothing but a series of enormous
shell-holes filled with pieces of uniform, weapons and dead bodies.
The ground all round, as far as the eye could see, was ploughed by
shells. You could search in vain for one wretched blade of grass.
This churned-up battlefield was ghastly. Among the living lay the
dead. As we dug ourselves in we found them in layers stacked on top
of each other. One company after another had been shoved into the
gaps and gone under.

---

On the beauty of battle:

Throughout the war, it was always my endeavour to view my opponent
without animus, and to form an opinion of him as a man on the basis
of the courage he showed. I would always try to seek him out in
combat and kill him, and I expected nothing different from him. But
never did I entertain mean thoughts of him.

---

On the meaning of the war:

In war you learn your lessons, and they stay learned, but the
tuition fees are high.

---

The final passage:

When once it is no longer possible to understand how a man gives his
life for his country -- and the time will come -- then all is over
with that faith also, and the idea of the Fatherland is dead; and
then, perhaps, we shall be envied, as we envy the saints their
deceitful ecstasy.

---

On human nature:

Human nature is indeed indestructible.

---

At the war's end, on receiving the Pour le Merite:

At the sight of the Neckar slopes wreathed with flowering cherry
trees, I could barely contain my emotion. I felt I had not yet
expressed my gratitude for all the great and small blessings that
had been granted me. For four years I had been part of a great
enterprise that demanded the ultimate sacrifice, and I had emerged
from it, despite the utmost danger, without serious damage to body
or soul. I had the feeling that life held something more in store
for me. The steel that had wounded me had in fact tempered me.

---

EDITORIAL NOTE FOR STUDENTS:

Junger found meaning, beauty, and even ecstasy in the same war that
destroyed Wilfred Owen (Source 32). They fought on opposite sides of
the same trenches. Neither is lying about what they experienced. The
question is not who is "right" -- it is what it means that the same
experience can produce such opposite conclusions.

Junger served as a German infantry officer for the entire war, was
wounded fourteen times, and was awarded Germany's highest military
honour. He lived to be 102, dying in 1998. He never repudiated the
book or the war. He also refused to join the Nazi Party.
