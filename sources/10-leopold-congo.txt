LEOPOLD II AND THE CONGO FREE STATE (1876-1908)
Selected Primary Sources
Sources: Project Gutenberg (EBook #50573 — The Casement Report); Project Gutenberg
(EBook #62739 — King Leopold's Soliloquy, Mark Twain); Internet Archive (Red Rubber,
E.D. Morel); One More Voice Project (Casement testimonies); Vancouver Island
University / Prof. Davies (British Consul Report excerpts); BlackPast.org (Williams
Open Letter); National Council for the Social Studies (Leopold speeches)

Between 1885 and 1908, King Leopold II of Belgium ruled the Congo Free State as his
personal property — not a Belgian colony, but a private fiefdom. Under the guise of
philanthropy and "civilisation," Leopold's regime extracted ivory and rubber through a
system of forced labour, hostage-taking, and terror. Conservative estimates place the
death toll at ten million people; some scholars estimate far higher. The regime became
the subject of the first major international human rights campaign, led by E.D. Morel
and Roger Casement.

These sources trace the arc from Leopold's public rhetoric of civilisation, through
the testimony of witnesses and victims, to the investigative reports that exposed the
reality. They are among the most important documents in the history of colonialism,
human rights, and the gap between what power says and what power does.

I. LEOPOLD'S PUBLIC RHETORIC

OPENING ADDRESS TO THE BRUSSELS GEOGRAPHIC CONFERENCE (12 September 1876)

Leopold organised an International Geographical Conference in Brussels, ostensibly to
coordinate European efforts to "open up" Central Africa. His speech set the template
for humanitarian imperialism — the language of civilisation masking the machinery of
extraction.

    To open up to civilization the only part of our globe which it has
    not yet penetrated, to pierce the darkness in which entire populations
    are enveloped, is, I venture to say, a crusade worthy of this age of
    progress, and I am happy to perceive how much the public feeling is
    in favor of its accomplishments; the tide is with us.

    Needless to say, in bringing you to Brussels, I have not been
    influenced by selfish views. No, gentlemen, if Belgium is small, she
    is happy and contented with her lot. I have no ambition other than
    to serve her.

II. LEOPOLD'S PRIVATE CALCULATIONS

LETTER TO PRIME MINISTER BEERNAERT ON THE CONGO (3 July 1890)

While publicly proclaiming altruism, Leopold was negotiating to bind Belgium to the
Congo enterprise. This letter reveals the commercial logic behind the humanitarian
facade. Leopold framed Belgium's annexation of the Congo as an economic opportunity,
leveraging the philanthropic language that had won him international support to secure
domestic political backing.

[The full text of this letter is held in Belgian state archives. Excerpts appear in
Adam Hochschild, "King Leopold's Ghost" (1998), and in teaching materials compiled
by the National Council for the Social Studies.]

III. GEORGE WASHINGTON WILLIAMS — OPEN LETTER TO KING LEOPOLD II
(Stanley Falls, 18 July 1890)

George Washington Williams (1849-1891) was an African-American soldier, minister,
lawyer, journalist, and the first major historian of Black Americans. In 1890 he
travelled to the Congo expecting to find Leopold's promised "civilising" enterprise.
Instead he found systematic brutality. His Open Letter — written from Stanley Falls
and addressed directly to Leopold — is regarded as one of the founding documents of
human rights journalism, and one of the earliest uses of the phrase "crimes against
humanity."

    Good and Great Friend,

    I have the honour to submit for your Majesty's consideration some
    reflections respecting the Independent State of Congo, based upon a
    careful study and inspection of the country and the personal
    Government you have established upon the African Continent ...

    Your Majesty's Government has been engaged in the slave trade,
    wholesale and retail. It has been the practice of your Majesty's
    Government to capture native women, and hold them as hostages until
    their husbands and relations bring in the required amount of rubber.

    The labour tax imposed upon the natives is most unjust ... The
    soldiers sent to collect it are brutal and cruel ... and compel them
    to obey at the point of the bayonet.

    Your Majesty's Government has sequestered their land, burned their
    towns, stolen their property, enslaved their women and children,
    and committed other crimes too numerous to mention in detail.

    All the crimes perpetrated in the Congo have been done in your name,
    and you must answer at the bar of Public Sentiment for the
    misgovernment of a people, whose lives and fortunes were entrusted
    to you by the august Conference of Berlin, 1884-1885.

[Williams enumerated twelve specific charges, including: mercenary soldiers extorting
food and burning villages; contract violations with workers whose letters home were
suppressed; courts that were "abortive, unjust, partial and delinquent"; excessive
cruelty to prisoners in chain gangs; women imported for "immoral purposes"; the state
engaging in trade while taxing competitors; firing on canoes and confiscating property
in violation of the Berlin Conference; and waging wars to secure slaves.]

IV. THE CASEMENT REPORT (1904)
Roger Casement, British Consul — Report to the Houses of Parliament

Roger Casement (1864-1916) was a British diplomat who travelled through the Congo
interior in 1903 to investigate reports of atrocities. His report, delivered to
Parliament on 12 February 1904, ran forty pages with twenty additional pages of
individual testimony. It documented systematic forced labour, hostage-taking,
mutilation, and mass depopulation. An independent Belgian commission of enquiry
subsequently confirmed his findings in every detail.

OPENING

    I have the honour to submit my Report on my recent journey on the
    Upper Congo.

FORCED LABOUR AND THE RUBBER TAX

    The natives of the districts are forced to provide a fixed quantity
    [of rubber] each week which is levied by requisitions on all the
    surrounding villages.

    Armed expeditions have been necessary and a more forcible method
    of levying supplies adopted than the law either contemplated or
    justifies.

    [Those who fled declared] that they had endured such ill-treatment
    at the hands of the Government officials and soldiers that nothing
    had remained but to be killed for failure to bring in rubber or to
    die in their attempts to satisfy the demands made upon them.

    From our country each village had to take twenty loads of rubber.
    We had to go further and further into the forest to find the rubber
    vines, to go without food, and our women had to give up cultivating
    the fields and gardens.

    The only way to get rubber is to fight for it.
        — Statement of a European official in the Congo

THE SENTRY AND HOSTAGE SYSTEM

Casement documented how the concessionary companies and the state posted armed
sentries in villages to enforce rubber quotas. Women and children were seized as
hostages to compel their husbands and fathers to collect rubber.

    A sentry in the employ of one of the concessionary private companies
    said he had caught and was detaining as prisoners eleven women to
    compel their husbands to bring in the right amount of rubber required
    of them on the next market day.

    If the Government plantation or the kitchen garden require weeding,
    a soldier will be sent to call in the women from some of the
    neighbouring towns.

THE HAND-CUTTING SYSTEM

The most notorious practice of the Congo Free State was the cutting off of hands.
Soldiers were issued cartridges and required to account for each one by producing a
severed right hand as proof that the bullet had been used to kill — not "wasted" on
hunting. In practice, soldiers often cut hands from the living to account for
cartridges used for other purposes.

    Each time the corporal goes out to get rubber, cartridges are given
    him. He must bring back all not used, and for every one used he must
    bring back a right hand.
        — From a diary quoted by Casement

    One, a young man, both of whose hands had been beaten off with the
    butt ends of rifles against a tree.

    [A boy of about fifteen years of age whose] left hand had been
    hacked off by the wrist, and ... a shot hole appeared in the fleshy
    part of the forearm. The boy ... said that a sentry of the La
    Lulanga Company now in the town had cut off his hand.

MUTILATION TESTIMONY — THE CASE OF V V (Lake Mantumba District)

From the testimony appended to the Casement Report, recorded by Casement during
his investigation. The witness's name was anonymised in the published report.

    [State soldiers under a European officer attacked towns including
    the witness's village.] Soldiers tied prisoners' hands tightly with
    rope during heavy rain, causing severe swelling. When the swelling
    cut into bone, soldiers beat his hands against a tree with rifle
    butts. His hands subsequently fell off.

    It was the deliberate act of the soldiers of a European
    Administration, and these men themselves never made any concealment
    that in committing these acts they were but obeying the positive
    orders of their superiors.

    [Casement also documented] a boy of approximately 12 years old with
    his right hand severed, [who] witnessed his parents being killed;
    [and] an elderly woman named V W who lost her hand during a military
    raid where her son was also killed.

POPULATION COLLAPSE

    This district had, when I visited it in 1887, numbered fully 5,000
    people; today the population is given, after a careful enumeration,
    at less than 600.

    The population of the lake-side towns would seem to have diminished
    within the last ten years by 60 or 70 percent.

Casement's appendix on the Lake Mantumba region provided village-by-village data:

    Botunu: from 500 to 80 residents
    Irebo: from 3,000 to 60 residents
    Ngero: from 2,500 to 300 residents

    Sixteen of these whose names were given to me were killed by the
    soldiers, and their bodies recovered by their friends.

PHYSICAL PUNISHMENT

    Several men, including a Chief of one town, showed broad weals
    across their buttocks, which were evidently recent.

DISEASE AND NEGLECT

    I found seventeen sleeping sickness patients, male and female,
    lying about in the utmost dirt.

    The population of the Lower Congo has been gradually reduced by
    the unchecked ravages of this, as yet undiagnosed and incurable
    disease.

V. E.D. MOREL — "RED RUBBER" (1906) AND "KING LEOPOLD'S RULE IN AFRICA" (1904)

Edmund Dene Morel (1873-1924) was a British journalist who, while working as a
shipping clerk in Antwerp, noticed that ships arriving from the Congo carried huge
quantities of rubber and ivory, while ships departing for the Congo carried only
guns, chains, and ammunition — no trade goods. He deduced that what Leopold called
"commerce" was in fact forced labour. Morel became the leading campaigner against
Leopold's Congo regime, founding the Congo Reform Association in 1904 and publishing
extensively.

From "Red Rubber" (1906):

    [The rubber system meant that] first came the command to build
    houses for the soldiers ... Then they were told to bring in rubber
    ... But soon the reward was reduced until at last they were told to
    bring in the rubber for nothing.

    A careful estimate made the population of, say, seven years ago,
    to be 2,000 people ... All told, they would not muster 200 now.

Morel published photographs of mutilated Congolese men, women, and children — images
that shocked European and American audiences and became central to the reform campaign.
His books documented the systematic use of hostage-taking, forced labour, punitive
military expeditions, and the amputation of hands as tools of the rubber extraction
system.

[The full texts of "Red Rubber" and "King Leopold's Rule in Africa" are available on
the Internet Archive: archive.org/details/redrubberstoryof00more and
archive.org/details/kingleopoldsrule00moreuoft]

VI. MARK TWAIN — "KING LEOPOLD'S SOLILOQUY" (1905)

Mark Twain's pamphlet is a satirical monologue in which Leopold defends his Congo
rule. Twain embeds real primary source material — missionary reports, diary entries,
and Casement's testimony — within the satirical frame. The pamphlet was published by
the Congo Reform Association and became one of the most widely circulated documents
of the reform campaign.

LEOPOLD ON HIS OWN POWER

    I have ruled the Congo State not as a trustee of the Powers, an
    agent, a subordinate, a foreman, but as a sovereign — sovereign
    over a fruitful domain four times as large as the German Empire —
    sovereign absolute, irresponsible, above all law.

    [The] rubber, the ivory and all the other riches of the land mine
    — mine solely — and gathered for me by the men, the women and the
    little children under compulsion of lash and bullet, fire,
    starvation, mutilation and the halter.

LEOPOLD ON HOW HE OBTAINED THE CONGO

    I went pilgriming among the Powers in tears, with my mouth full of
    Bible and my pelt oozing piety at every pore.

LEOPOLD ON HIS CRITICS

    I do not see how a person can act so. This Morel is a king's
    subject, and reverence for monarchy should have restrained him.

EMBEDDED MISSIONARY TESTIMONY: REV. A.E. SCRIVENER (1903)

    Bang! bang! bang! and down fell one and another, dead, in the
    graveyard of their own homes.

    [Near a settlement he found] thirty-six skulls, and saw many sets
    of bones.

EMBEDDED TESTIMONY: REV. W.H. SHEPPARD

After a punitive raid, a chief reported killing "between eighty and ninety" people
in a fenced enclosure. Sheppard witnessed eighty-one severed hands being dried over
a fire for official verification.

LEOPOLD ON THE POPULATION DECLINE

Twain has Leopold defensively respond to critics who calculate "the reduction of
that Congo population from 25,000,000 to 15,000,000 in the twenty years of my
administration."

THE COMMISSION OF ENQUIRY (quoted by Twain)

    Imprisonment of women hostages, the subjection of the chiefs to
    servile labour ... were the rule commonly followed.

[Full text available at Project Gutenberg: gutenberg.org/ebooks/62739]

VII. CONGOLESE WITNESS TESTIMONY — LAKE MANTUMBA REGION (1904)

From the appendices to the Casement Report, these are statements by five anonymised
Congolese witnesses (identified as Q Q, R R, S S, T T, and U U) describing their
experiences during the "rubber wars" that began in 1893.

    They killed her with a gun — they shot her through the stomach.

    [Witnesses described] soldiers pursuing families into bushland;
    extrajudicial killings of prisoners; forced labour conscription;
    separation of family members; starvation and disease in military
    camps.

    [Soldiers] collected severed hands as proof of duty.

[Transcriptions available at One More Voice: onemorevoice.org/html/transcriptions/
liv_020058_TEI.html and liv_020059_TEI.html]

NOTE ON AUTHENTICITY

One document sometimes attributed to Leopold — a "Letter to Colonial Missionaries"
dated 1883, beginning "Your essential role is to facilitate the task of administrators
and industrials" — is widely circulated online but its provenance is disputed by
historians. It does not appear in the Belgian state archives and its earliest
traceable source is a 1935 claim by Moukouani Muikwani Bukoko. Scholars such as
Adam Hochschild have noted that while the sentiments may reflect Leopold's actual
views, the document itself cannot be verified as authentic. It has been excluded
from this collection for that reason.

All other documents in this file are established primary sources available through
major digital archives (Project Gutenberg, Internet Archive, British Parliamentary
Papers, One More Voice Project).
