LETTERS AND ACCOUNTS FROM CHINESE RAILROAD WORKERS IN AMERICA (1860s-1870s)

EDITORIAL NOTE:

This is one of the most difficult primary sources in this collection to find in
full text. No personal letters, diaries, or writings authored by Chinese workers
on the first transcontinental railroad have been found. As the Stanford Chinese
Railroad Workers in North America Project (the largest study of 19th-century
Chinese American history) notes, the voices of the approximately 15,000-20,000
Chinese workers who built the western portion of the Transcontinental Railroad
have been almost entirely lost.

The reasons for this silence are multiple: most workers were illiterate or
semi-literate laborers from rural Guangdong Province; letters sent home (qiaopi)
were written on fragile paper and rarely preserved; anti-Chinese discrimination
meant their stories were not recorded by English-language media; and the
Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 created an atmosphere in which Chinese Americans
had reason to destroy documents rather than preserve them.

What follows are the closest available primary source materials.

==========================================================================

DOCUMENT 1: A CHINESE IMMIGRANT RECALLS THE DANGERS OF RAILROAD WORK

Source: "Chinese American Voices" -- a retrospective account from 1926 in which
an elderly Chinese immigrant recalls conditions during 1860s-1880s railroad
construction.

The following account describes the life-threatening hazards Chinese workers
faced, including dynamite blasting through the Sierra Nevada mountains, harsh
winter conditions where workers were buried alive in snow avalanches, and the
dangerous practice of being lowered in baskets over cliff faces to drill holes
for explosives.

[The full text of this account is held at the American Social History Project /
Center for Media and Learning (ASHP/CML), CUNY Graduate Center:
https://shec.ashp.cuny.edu/items/show/614]

==========================================================================

DOCUMENT 2: QIAOPI (LETTERS WITH REMITTANCES)

Qiaopi are letters sent by overseas Chinese workers to their families back home,
typically containing both a personal letter and a monetary remittance. They were
most often addressed to parents or grandparents, as most women and children
could not read. The UNESCO Memory of the World Register recognized qiaopi
collections in 2013 for their historical significance.

Sample qiaopi folk verses preserved in oral tradition:

"A full boat of tears, a full boat of men;
One travels overseas just with a scrap of a wash-cloth."

"A piece of qiaopi, two yuan of note.
Asking my wife: to work hard and do not worry..."

These verses capture the emotional reality of separation: young men leaving
their families for years of dangerous labor abroad, sending money home with
brief letters that had to convey love, reassurance, and practical instructions
in very few words.

DOCUMENT 3: THE CENTRAL PACIFIC RAILROAD'S OWN RECORDS

While the Chinese workers' own words are largely lost, their presence is
documented in the records of the Central Pacific Railroad Company. Charles
Crocker, one of the "Big Four" railroad owners, testified:

"They are very trusty, they are very intelligent, and they live up to their
contracts."

James Strobridge, the construction superintendent who initially opposed hiring
Chinese workers, later acknowledged their essential role:

"[They were] the best workers in the world."

A petition from Chinese railroad workers in June 1867, during their famous
strike for higher wages and shorter hours, survives in paraphrase. The workers
demanded:
- $40 per month (equal to white workers' pay, up from $30)
- A 10-hour workday in the open, 8 hours in tunnels
- An end to the right of overseers to whip workers
- Freedom to leave and seek other employment

The strike was broken after a week when Crocker cut off food supplies to the
remote mountain camps.

NOTE FOR EDUCATORS:

The absence of Chinese workers' own voices is itself a primary source -- it
teaches students about whose stories get preserved and whose get erased. The
Stanford project has worked to recover these voices through:
- Archaeological excavation of worker campsites
- Chinese-language newspaper accounts
- Payroll records and company correspondence
- Oral histories from descendants
- Qiaopi collections in Guangdong Province archives
