HIBAKUSHA TESTIMONIES
First-Person Accounts from Survivors of the Atomic Bombing of Hiroshima
August 6, 1945

Sources: The Voice of Hibakusha project (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK),
translated by college students under advisor Laurence Wiig, archived at
atomicarchive.com; the Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum; Hibakusha Stories
project (hibakushastories.org); the Nobel Prize Foundation; History Hit;
PBS; ICRC.

================================================================================

1. SETSUKO THURLOW (age 13)
   Location: Army headquarters building, central Hiroshima
   Source: Nobel Peace Prize Acceptance Speech, Oslo, December 10, 2017;
   Hibakusha Stories project (hibakushastories.org)

I speak as a member of the family of hibakusha -- those of us who, by some
miraculous chance, survived the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki.
For more than seven decades, we have worked for the total abolition of nuclear
weapons.

I was just 13 years old when the United States dropped the first atomic bomb,
on my city Hiroshima. I still vividly remember that morning. At 8:15, I saw a
blinding bluish-white flash from the window. I remember having the sensation
of floating in the air.

As I regained consciousness in the silence and darkness, I found myself pinned
by the collapsed building. I began to hear my classmates' faint cries: "Mother,
help me. God, help me."

Then, suddenly, I felt hands touching my left shoulder, and heard a man saying:
"Don't give up! Keep pushing! I am trying to free you. See the light coming
through that opening? Crawl toward it and try to get out!"

As I crawled out, the ruins were on fire. Most of my classmates in that building
were burned to death alive. I saw all around me utter, unimaginable devastation.

Processions of ghostly figures shuffled by. Grotesquely wounded people, they
were bleeding, burnt, blackened and swollen. Parts of their bodies were missing.
Flesh and skin hung from their bones. Some with their eyeballs hanging in their
hands. Some with their bellies burst open, their intestines hanging out. The
foul stench of burnt human flesh filled the air.

Thus, with one bomb my beloved city was obliterated. Most of its residents were
civilians who were incinerated, vaporized, carbonized -- among them, members of
my own family and 351 of my schoolmates.

In the weeks, months and years that followed, many thousands more would die,
often in random and mysterious ways, from the delayed effects of radiation.
Still to this day, radiation is killing survivors.

Whenever I remember Hiroshima, the first image that comes to mind is of my
four-year-old nephew, Eiji -- his little body transformed into an unrecognizable
melted chunk of flesh. He kept begging for water in a faint voice until his
death released him from agony.

To me, he came to represent all the innocent children of the world, threatened
as they are at this very moment by nuclear weapons. Every second of every day,
nuclear weapons endanger everyone we love and everything we hold dear. We must
not tolerate this insanity any longer.

Through our agony and the sheer struggle to survive -- and to rebuild our lives
from the ashes -- we hibakusha became convinced that we must warn the world about
these apocalyptic weapons. Time and again, we shared our testimonies.

When I was a 13-year-old girl, trapped in the smouldering rubble, I kept pushing.
I kept moving toward the light. And I survived. Our light now is the ban treaty.
To all in this hall and all listening around the world, I repeat those words that
I heard called to me in the ruins of Hiroshima: "Don't give up! Keep pushing!
See the light? Crawl towards it."

2. AKIHIRO TAKAHASHI (age 14)
   Location: 1.4 km from hypocenter
   Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

We were about to form lines facing the front, we saw a B-29 approaching. The
blast struck. I was knocked approximately 10 meters.

My clothing was destroyed by heat. I suffered severe burns on the back of the
head, on my back, on both arms and both legs, with skin peeling and hanging.

Walking westward toward home, I encountered a severely burned friend named
Yamamoto. Together we navigated past horrific scenes of other victims before
reaching the river, where we narrowly escaped an approaching firestorm.

After 18 months of medical treatment, I survived -- but out of sixty of my
junior high school classmates, only ten of us are alive today. My two closest
friends died from acute radiation disease.

I developed keloids on both hands, restricted movement in my right arm, and
carry a permanent reminder -- a black nail on my fourth finger where embedded
glass destroyed the nail's root cells.

We survivors must continue to talk about our experiences, to honor those
who perished.

================================================================================

3. YOSHITAKA KAWAMOTO (age 13)
   Location: Classroom, 0.8 km from hypocenter
   Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

A classmate noticed a B-29 approaching. As I stood up, all I can remember was
a pale lightening flash for two or three seconds. Then, I collapsed.

Buried under debris, I heard my classmates singing their school song, hoping
for rescue. I eventually freed myself and discovered the scale of destruction
-- a dark sky with a tornado or a big fire ball storming throughout the city.

I found a mortally injured classmate whose skull was cracked open, his flesh
was dangling out from his head. Unable to help, I fled as desperate hands
grabbed my ankles. I reached Miyuki Bridge seeking water but found corpses.
The water was dead people.

Soldiers collecting bodies found my pulse still beating and transported me with
survivors rather than the deceased. Hair loss, blindness, and weakness followed,
but my youth enabled recovery within a year.

[Kawamoto later became director of the Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum.]

4. AKIKO TAKAKURA (age 20)
   Location: Bank of Hiroshima, 300 meters from hypocenter
   Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

I saw a white magnesium flash before losing consciousness. Upon regaining
awareness in darkness, I found my coworker Ms. Asami crying. Despite injuries,
I helped revive Ms. Asami using water from exploded pipes and assisted her
escape.

A whirlpool of fire approached from the street -- it was like a big tornado
of fire. The intense heat caused burns to my ear and leg. Then came the black
rain with big drops, though it failed to quench our severe thirst.

I saw deceased victims whose fingertips caught fire, bodies burned and deformed.
I sustained over 100 lacerated wounds on my back but miraculously survived
despite being so close to the hypocenter.

For years afterward, I remained terribly afraid of fire. I struggled with
sensory memories of the heat, smoke, and difficulty breathing.

[Takakura later ran a kindergarten and shared her experience with children.]

5. KINUE TOMOYASU (age 44)
   Location: 5 km from hypocenter; daughter was at a station in the city
   Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

That morning I left home with my daughter. She took the train and when she
got off at the station, she was hit by the A-bomb. After returning home, I
experienced the blast. As I opened the window, there came the flash. The flash
was piercing my eyes and my mind went blank.

I witnessed devastation while traveling toward Hiroshima, encountering an
injured girl and crowds of casualties. It was like a heavy rain -- it fell on
my hands. The black rain.

At dawn on August 7th, a neighbor revealed my daughter's location at the river
bank. Upon arrival, my daughter said: "There shouldn't be any war."

I held her on my lap. She said, "I don't want to die." Nine hours later, my
daughter died.

I developed radiation sickness with hair loss and purple spots. My son returned
from war but eventually killed himself by jumping in front of a train in October.

If it were not for the war, my two children would not have died.

6. AKIRA ONOGI (age 16)
   Location: 1.2 km from ground zero
   Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

I was reading lying on the floor with a friend of mine. Under the eaves I
saw blue flash of light just like a spark made by a train or some short circuit.

The blast threw both of us into another room, leaving me unconscious briefly.
I dug through debris to rescue my mother, three sisters, and a niece. I
witnessed a neighbor whose skin was peeling off all over his body and was
hanging from finger tips.

We found a trapped woman pinned by a fallen beam at a bridge. We couldn't
remove it without tools and were forced to abandon her as fire approached.

About one hour later, it started raining heavily. There were large drops of
black rain.

The river was choked with debris and corpses. I witnessed injured survivors
and a young boy with his right leg cut at the thigh, hopping across a bridge.

For roughly a decade afterward, I avoided train sparks and windows due to
glass injury concerns, sitting with the wall behind me as a self-protective
instinct.

7. HATCHOBORI STREETCAR SURVIVORS
   Location: Hatchobori area, 750 meters from ground zero
   Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

   Multiple passengers on the same streetcar gave testimony:

   TOMIKO SASAKI (age 17):

There was the flash and darkness. I think I was unconscious for a while.

[She aided her burned classmates with water from a fire tank. Her friends
died within two weeks.]

   EIKO TAOKA (age 21):

[She was holding her infant son when the blast occurred.] He looked at my
face and smiled.

[Her son died of radiation sickness on August 28. She believed his death
was a sacrifice for her survival.]

   KEIKO MATSUDA (age 14):

It was very, very hot. I touched my skin and it just peeled right off.

[She developed severe complications including maggots in her eyes and
erysipelas.]

   TAKEO WATANABE (age 16):

[Suffered lasting speech difficulties from cerebral thrombosis. He had
avoided discussing his experience until this testimony.]

   AKIRA ISHIDA (age 17):

My hair is growing back!

[He recalled his mother's joy when his hair regrew. He emphasized rebirth
following atomic devastation.]

   TSUTAICHI MATSUZAKA (age 37):

I pray that there never be another nuclear war like that.

[He experienced severe radiation effects including hair loss and fever.
Three of his coworkers all perished within weeks.]

   SHIZUNO TOCHIKI (age 23):

[After the "Pika-don" (flash-bang) blast, she endured radiation sickness
with purple spots and hair loss. Recovery took a month, during which she
struggled to walk.]

8. MAMORU YUKIHIRO (age 36)
   Location: Agricultural office, approximately 1 km from city center
   Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

When the bomb struck, we were all in the big room. We'd just finished the
morning gathering of all the employees at around 8:05 a.m. The destruction
was instantaneous -- as if a box of matches has suddenly been struck by a
hammer and crushed to pieces.

Despite suffering wounds, I chose to rescue colleagues rather than rush home
to my family, believing that if I had helped those people, God would help my
family. I spent about an hour breaking through a 7-centimeter board to free
trapped coworkers. Seventy-four bank employees perished.

One month later, I developed red spots and was found to have dangerously low
white blood cell counts -- 1,200 versus 6,000 normal. My wife developed
uterine cancer in 1949.

My daughter, exposed at age four, developed severe anemia and hemorrhaging.
She became the first publicly documented A-bomb survivor with radiation-caused
internal disease. Finally, at the beginning of February of 1954, she died.

9. HIROSHI SAWACHIKA (age 28)
   Location: Army medical post, 4.1 km from hypocenter
   Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

I experienced a sudden bright red light and intense heat while in my office.
I felt weightless as if I were an astronaut before losing consciousness
briefly. When I regained awareness, everyone in the room had been thrown to
one side, with furniture and window frames scattered.

I directed evacuations and noticed the mushroom cloud forming over the gas
company. Though my shirt became stained with a colleague's blood, I myself
sustained no serious injuries.

Hundreds of injured citizens arrived at headquarters seeking treatment. I and
other medical personnel treated approximately 2,000 to 3,000 patients that
day, focusing on first aid given the lack of facilities and supplies.

One incident remained with me -- a pregnant woman who accepted her imminent
death but pleaded for her baby to be saved. When I returned hours later, she
had died. This encounter symbolized for me the tragedy's human dimension.

10. ISAO KITA (age 33)
    Location: Hiroshima District Weather Bureau, 3.7 km from hypocenter
    Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

I noticed the flashing light while receiving wireless transmissions, then
experienced an intense heat wave. I observed white clouds spreading across
the sky and heard cracking sounds as air expanded in the room.

Following emergency training, I lay with my face on the floor and counted
seconds. At the five-second mark, I heard groaning and the blast shattered
windows. My measurements showed the blast traveled approximately 700 meters
per second -- twice the speed of sound.

I witnessed injured people streaming toward a hospital, many bleeding and
unclothed. The city turned into a yellow sand.

I observed dramatic visual contrasts -- bright sunlight on one side while
thick smoke created darkness on the other. I observed the black rain falling
elsewhere that was sticky and wouldn't wash off.

The bomb kills everyone from little babies to old people. It is a very cruel
and very painful way to die. People all over the world must speak out.

11. HIROKO FUKADA (age 18)
    Location: Bureau of Post Communications, 1 km from ground zero
    Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

There was a very strong flash of light, followed by tremendous impact.
Pika-don -- spark and bang.

After exiting the building, I witnessed horrific conditions. Everybody was
terribly injured. We were even embarrassed because we were not injured.

I was swept into the river by crowds and encountered a tornado while crossing.
The water was swirling around me and later I learned that was a tornado.

In the water, I found my friend's leg. That was actually my friend's leg. And
she was alive and we were so glad to see each other.

My mother died August 10 at a first aid center. My younger sister was never
found after being mobilized to demolish houses. My two younger brothers
survived -- one under building eaves, another escaping a collapsed school.

At 18, I suddenly became guardian to my brothers. I didn't know what to do.
But I had two small brothers that I had to take care of and support.

It is hard to talk about it. I can't.

================================================================================

12. TAEKO TERAMAE (age 15)
    Location: Central telephone office, 0.5 km from hypocenter
    Source: HIROSHIMA WITNESS (Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center / NHK)

There was a flash, far brighter than one used for a camera, and I heard
buildings collapse around me. Trapped under debris, I experienced
disorientation and nausea from sulfur-like smells.

My teacher, Mr. Wakita, helped me escape by swimming across a burning river
despite my fading vision.

I initially didn't realize the extent of my facial wounds. Later, discovering
via mirror reflection that my left eye was severely damaged alongside cuts
to my right eye, nose, and jaw, I experienced profound shock about my changed
appearance.

I hope to support the isolated survivors -- particularly those who lost
families or remained single due to bomb-related injuries. Many survivors
expressed longing to simply regain their health, even temporarily.

13. SUNAO TSUBOI (age 20)
    Location: En route to university, central Hiroshima
    Source: History Hit; PBS; ICRC
    [Tsuboi died in 2021, aged 96. He served as co-chair of Nihon Hidankyo,
    the Japan-wide organization of atomic bomb sufferers.]

There was a blinding flash. My first thought was that it must have been a
large bomb of the type we'd been warned about, an incendiary or napalm. As
I lay there wondering what had happened, a huge blast ripped through the air.
It lifted me off the ground and hurled me 10 meters.

When I regained consciousness, my body was covered in severe burns. The skin
on my left arm was hanging down in strips like seaweed.

There were people crying out for help, calling after members of their family.
I saw a schoolgirl with her eye hanging out of its socket. People looked like
ghosts, bleeding and trying to walk before collapsing.

Despite burns covering 25 percent of my body, I assisted in rescue efforts,
carrying injured victims to a makeshift hospital. In the following weeks, as
I lay in agony, my 13-year-old sister died from radiation exposure.

[Tsuboi channeled his suffering into activism, working tirelessly for nuclear
disarmament for over seven decades. In 2016, he met President Obama during
Obama's historic visit to Hiroshima.]

NOTE ON SOURCES AND CONTEXT

The testimonies numbered 2-12 are from the program HIROSHIMA WITNESS, produced
by the Hiroshima Peace Cultural Center and NHK (the public broadcasting company
of Japan). They were translated into English by a group of college students
under the supervision of advisor Laurence Wiig, and are archived at
atomicarchive.com (The Voice of Hibakusha collection). The index of all 15
testimonies is at:
  https://www.atomicarchive.com/resources/documents/hibakusha/index.html

Setsuko Thurlow's testimony (no. 1) is from her Nobel Peace Prize acceptance
speech on behalf of ICAN (International Campaign to Abolish Nuclear Weapons),
delivered in Oslo on December 10, 2017. Full text archived at wagingpeace.org
and the Nobel Prize Foundation.

Sunao Tsuboi's testimony (no. 13) is compiled from accounts published by
History Hit, PBS, and the International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC).

Additional hibakusha testimonies in the HIROSHIMA WITNESS collection not
included here: Takehiko Sakai, Toshiko Saeki, Yosaku Mikami, and Yoshito
Matsushige (the photographer who took the only known photographs of Hiroshima
on the day of the bombing).

The Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum maintains an extensive database of survivor
video testimonies with English subtitles at:
  https://hpmm-db.jp/testify_en/

The hibakusha movement was awarded the 2024 Nobel Peace Prize through the
organization Nihon Hidankyo (Japan Confederation of A- and H-Bomb Sufferers
Organizations), founded in 1956.

For further reading:
  - John Hersey, "Hiroshima" (The New Yorker, August 31, 1946; later expanded
    into a book)
  - James Yamazaki, "Children of the Atomic Bomb" (1995)
  - "Hibakusha: Survivors of Hiroshima and Nagasaki," translated by Gaynor
    Sekimori (Kosei Publishing, 1989) -- 25 first-hand accounts

On the morning of August 6, 1945, the United States dropped an atomic bomb
(nicknamed "Little Boy") on Hiroshima, Japan. The bomb detonated at 8:15 a.m.
approximately 600 meters above the city. An estimated 70,000 to 80,000 people
were killed instantly, and by the end of 1945, the death toll had risen to
approximately 140,000. The survivors -- hibakusha -- and their descendants
continued to suffer from radiation-related illness for decades afterward.

The word "hibakusha" literally means "explosion-affected person." Under
Japanese law, hibakusha are recognized as a special category requiring medical
and social support. As of 2024, approximately 106,000 hibakusha were still
alive, with an average age of over 85.
