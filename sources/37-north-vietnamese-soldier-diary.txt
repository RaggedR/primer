DANG THUY TRAM, EXCERPTS FROM "LAST NIGHT I DREAMED OF PEACE"
(Nhat Ky Dang Thuy Tram)
Written 1968-1970; published in Vietnam 2005; English translation
by Andrew X. Pham, Harmony Books (Random House), 2007.

NOTE: This diary remains under copyright. The excerpts below are
drawn from publicly available quotations, published reviews (NPR,
Kirkus, BookPage, Publishers Weekly), the Penguin Random House
preview, academic sources, and the Vietnam Center at Texas Tech
University. They are presented here for educational use under fair
use/fair dealing.

BACKGROUND:

Dang Thuy Tram (November 26, 1942 -- June 22, 1970) was a
27-year-old doctor from Hanoi who volunteered to serve as a
battlefield surgeon for the People's Army of Vietnam and the
National Liberation Front (Viet Cong) in Quang Ngai province,
in the central highlands of South Vietnam. Fresh from Hanoi
Medical University, she ran a field clinic treating wounded
soldiers and civilians under constant American bombardment.

She kept two diaries from April 1968 until June 20, 1970 --
two days before she was killed in a firefight at the age of 27.

After her death, her diaries were captured by American forces.
Frederick Whitehurst, a 22-year-old U.S. military intelligence
specialist, was ordered to burn them. A South Vietnamese
translator, Nguyen Trung Hieu, read a passage and told
Whitehurst: "Don't burn this. It has fire in it already."
Whitehurst kept the diaries for 35 years.

In 2005, with help from American veteran Ted Engelmann, the
diaries were returned to Tram's family in Hanoi. Published
in Vietnam that year, they sold over 430,000 copies and became
a national phenomenon. The English translation followed in
2007 under the title "Last Night I Dreamed of Peace."

Tram has been compared to Anne Frank -- a young woman writing
with fierce intelligence and honesty while trapped in a war
that would kill her, never knowing her private words would
reach the world.

========================================================================
DIARY ENTRIES
========================================================================

APRIL 8, 1968

Operated on one case of appendicitis with inadequate anesthesia
... just a few tubes of Novocain. A whirl of emotions unsettled
me.

---

APRIL 10, 1968

I cried so hard I could not face all your farewells ... I'll
see you again one day in our beloved North.

---

APRIL 12, 1968

Afternoon in the forest, the rain has left the leaves wet and
fragile, pale and lucid in the sunbeams, these emerald hands
of a maiden imprisoned within a forbidden fortress. The air
has gone somberly sad. In the patient ward, silence broods.
Murmurs of Huong's conversation drift from the staff's room.
An immense longing envelops me. Whom do I miss? Dad, Mom,
people who left ... and a patient waiting for me to come to
him. Within this longing roosts a secret and profound sorrow,
silent as this air, heavy as this earth. I feel the wound in
my heart still bleeds, an excruciating pain that neither work
nor memories can numb.

---

APRIL 13, 1968

So many letters come from all over. Thank you all for
showering me with such warm affection. I read your letters
with both joy and sadness. Why can everyone else love me so,
but the man who has my faithful heart cannot? Isn't that sad,
M.? I want to fill the emptiness in my soul with the affection
within these kind letters, but it is impossible. My heart beats
stubbornly with the tempo of a twenty-year-old, full of love
and affection. Oh, be calm my heart, seek the peaceful rhythm
of the sea on a windless afternoon.

---

APRIL 15, 1968

Never! I must do my best for San as well as for other patients!
Isn't that a physician's proud duty?

---

APRIL 26, 1968

My heart is heavy as I read brother Tam's letter sent from
H818 -- not a letter from M. The past is gone, why did you
remind me of it, Tam? You feel bad for me, but you
unintentionally hurt me ... it would have been better if you
had talked with me about important things, my work, my
awareness -- and no more.

---

APRIL 30, 1968

Why are you still sad, Thuy? The critically wounded soldiers
survive. Didn't the wan smiles on their pale faces bring you
happiness? ... And yet you are still sad. This sorrow has
seeped far into my heart like the relentless monsoon rain
willing itself deep into the earth.

---

MAY 1, 1968

Once more, we celebrate Labor Day in the jungle -- it is a
long, silent day, submerged in longings. I miss Hanoi, Dad,
Mom, and my siblings terribly. I doze off at noon and find
myself coming back to see Mom and my sisters in the
Supplementary School for Public Health Officers, down that
narrow road, to that gate at Mr. Nghiep's house; I slip
through the broken lattices of the gate as easily, as happily
as I did in those younger carefree days.

---

JUNE 2, 1968

Rain this afternoon, all the rain on the roof thatch and on
the tree leaves make a monotonous sound which saddens me.

---

JULY 25, 1968

Oh, my God. How hateful the war is. And the more hate, the
more the devils are eager to fight. Why do they enjoy shooting
and killing good people like us? How can they have the heart
to kill all those youngsters who love life, who are struggling
and living for so many hopes?

---

UNDATED, 1968

He looks as if he has been roasted in an oven.

[Note: Written about a patient with phosphorous burns from an
American incendiary weapon.]

---

UNDATED, 1968-69

For the first time I dig a grave to bury a comrade. The shovel
hits a rock, and sparks fly like the flame of hatred in my
heart.

---

UNDATED, 1968-69

The trust stemming from ten years of waiting and longing does
not erode easily, but when it cracks it's hard to repair.

I know the roots of my love still lie deep within my heart,
dormant but not dead. It can sprout, it can grow if spring
returns.

---

UNDATED, 1969

The hand-basket is heavy, but my worries are much heavier.
Hatred is bruising my liver, blackening my gut. This war has
robbed me of all my dreams of love. Oh! Cruel American
bandits, your crimes are piling up like a mountain. As long
as I live I vow to fight until my last drop of blood in this
thousand-year vendetta.

---

JUNE, 1969

I will not be there when they sing the victory song.

---

UNDATED, 1968-70

A person's most valuable possession is life. We only live
once; we must live so as not to sorely regret the months and
years lived wastefully, not to be ashamed of the months and
years lived wastefully, so that when we die we can say, "All
my life and all my strength have been dedicated to the most
noble goal in life, the struggle to liberate the human race."

[Note: This passage paraphrases Nikolai Ostrovsky's novel
"How the Steel Was Tempered" (1934), a Soviet classic widely
read in North Vietnam. That Tram wrote it in her diary as
personal conviction -- not as quotation -- tells you
something about how deeply the revolutionary education had
taken root in young Vietnamese of her generation.]

---

UNDATED, 1968-70

Even if I must sacrifice my life, there is nothing to regret.
You must be worthy of each day you are still alive. Honor is
an invaluable gem; do not let anyone tread on it, no matter
who he is or how powerful.

---

UNDATED, 1968-70

My soul is as full, as tumultuous, as a river after days of
torrential rain. My youth has been soaked with the sweat,
tears, blood, and bones of the living and the dead.

---

UNDATED, 1968-70

Last night I dreamed of Peace. Oh, the dream of Peace and
Independence has burned in the hearts of thirty million people
for so long. For Peace and Independence, we have sacrificed
everything. So many people have volunteered to sacrifice their
whole lives for these two words: Independence and Liberty. I,
too, have sacrificed my life for that grandiose fulfillment.

---

UNDATED, 1968-70

Life must pass through storms but should not bow before storms.

[Doi phai trai qua giong to nhung khong duoc cui dau truoc
giong to.]

---

JUNE 20, 1970 (FINAL ENTRY)

I am no longer a child. I have grown up ... But somehow at
this moment, I yearn deeply for Mom's caring hand ... Come to
me, squeeze my hand, know my loneliness, and give me the love,
the strength to prevail on the perilous road before me.

[Note: Two days after writing this, on June 22, 1970, Dang
Thuy Tram was killed in a firefight. She was 27 years old.]

========================================================================
EDITORIAL NOTE FOR STUDENTS
========================================================================

Read this diary alongside Wilfred Owen (Source 32) and Ernst
Junger (Source 33). All three writers were young, educated
people who went to war believing in something, and wrote about
what they found there.

But notice the differences:

Owen was a British officer who came to see the war as
meaningless slaughter. His poems are protests.

Junger was a German officer who found meaning and even beauty
in combat. His memoir celebrates the warrior's experience.

Tram was a doctor, not a soldier. She did not fight -- she
healed. Her enemy was not abstract. American planes bombed her
clinic. She treated phosphorous burns. She buried friends.
And yet her diary is not primarily about war. It is about
loneliness, love, family, professional duty, and the struggle
to remain a good person under impossible conditions.

She also wrote within a political framework -- the Communist
Party, revolutionary duty, hatred of "American bandits." Some
of her language is propaganda. Some of it is genuine fury at
watching her patients die from weapons she could not stop.
The challenge for the reader is to hear both registers at
once: the political language she was taught, and the human
voice breaking through it.

The question is not whether she was "right" about the war.
The question is: what does it mean to read a dead enemy's
private diary and recognize yourself in it?

That is exactly what Frederick Whitehurst understood when he
refused to burn these pages.

The original diaries are archived at the Vietnam Center and
Sam Johnson Vietnam Archive at Texas Tech University, Lubbock,
Texas: https://www.vietnam.ttu.edu/resources/tram_diary/
