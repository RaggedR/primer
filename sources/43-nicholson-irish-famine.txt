ASENATH NICHOLSON, "ANNALS OF THE FAMINE IN IRELAND" (1851)
Selected Chapters and Excerpts
Source: Library Ireland (www.libraryireland.com)

Asenath Hatch Nicholson (1792-1855) was an American teacher, reformer, abolitionist,
and vegetarian from Vermont who first traveled to Ireland in 1844 to investigate the
condition of the Irish poor. She returned in December 1846 as a relief worker during
the Great Famine (1845-1852) and spent the next several years distributing food and
aid across the country. Her "Annals of the Famine in Ireland, in 1847, 1848, and
1849" (published 1851) is a first-hand account of the suffering she witnessed.

Unlike many contemporary accounts that focused on logistics and policy, Nicholson
focused on the human face of suffering, combining documentary prose with dramatic
scenes and dialogues.

CHAPTER: APPEARANCE OF THE PEOPLE

The hotel keeper was in the habit of collecting a few shillings from lodgers
and travelers, and distributing them in pennies, to the starving, in the
morning. These recipients were as ravenous as hungry lions and tigers, as
void of reason, and more disgusting to the sight. If man is to be guided
by reason, then when reason is extinct, upon what can he fall back? If the
instinct that is planted in man is the image of God, in which He is made,
then where this God is extinguished there can be nothing but a wreck -- a
mass of neither man nor brute; for if he have lost the image of God, and
has not the instinct of animals, he stands out an unnatural growth, to be
wondered at rather than admired. I could scarcely believe that these
creatures were my fellow-beings. Never had I seen slaves so degraded; and
here I learnt that there are many pages in the volume of slavery, and that
every branch of it proceeds from one and the same root, though it assumes
different shapes. These poor creatures are in as virtual bondage to their
landlords and superiors as is possible for mind or body to be. They cannot
work unless they bid them; they cannot eat unless they feed them; and they
cannot get away unless they help them.

CHAPTER: MANNER OF BURYING THE STARVING

In the morning, when the sun was rising, we ascended a hill, among the
desolate cabins, where once was the song of health, and where far off in
the west, the sea stretched wide, and the variegated clouds gilded with the
morning sun, were dipping apparently in its waters.

This, said a daughter of the family, was once a pretty and a grand spot;
here, two years ago, these desolate fields were cultivated, and content and
cheerfulness were in every cabin. Now, from morning to night they wander in
search of a turnip, or go to the sea for sea-weed to boil, and often have
we found a corpse at the door, that the brother you see "might put a board
on 'em."

We have often seen an ass passing our window carrying a corpse, wound about
with some old remnant of a blanket or sheet; and thus, flung across its
back, a father or mother, wife or husband, was carried to the grave.

Sometimes, when the corpse was a little child, or it might be more than
one, they were put into a couple of baskets, and thus balanced upon the
sides of the ass, this melancholy hearse proceeded on without a friend to
follow it, but the one who was guiding the beast.

These burials tell more of the paralysing effects of famine than anything
else can do; for the Irish in all ages, have been celebrated for their
attention to the interment of their dead, sparing no expense.

When I stood in the burying-ground in that parish, I saw the brown silken
hair of a young girl, waving gently through a little cleft of stones, that
lay loosely upon her young breast. They had not room to put her beneath the
surface, but slightly, and a little green grass was pulled and spread over,
and then covered with stones. I never shall forget it.

In some parts the soil was manured with the slain. When the famine first
commenced, efforts were made to procure coffins; but the distress became so
great to the living, that every penny that could be procured must be given
for food; and the famished relatives, at last, were grateful if some hand
stronger than theirs would dig the pit, and put down the uncoffined body.

One Sabbath, when I was in Erris, the day was so stormy that the church
service was suspended. A barefooted woman, who one year before had called
to sell milk and kept a fine dairy, came into the house where I was, and
calling me by name, said: "Will ye give me something to buy a coffin to
put on my husband; he died yesterday on me, and it would be a pity to put
him in the ground without a board, and he is so swelled, ma'am, not a
ha'porth of his legs or belly but is ready to burst, and but a
fivepence-halfpenny could I gather, and the little boys are ashamed to go
out and ask the charity for him."

This is an illustration not only of the state into which famine has thrown
the country, but the apathy of feeling which the most tender-hearted people
on the globe manifested. A woman compelled to go out in a most perilous
storm, upon a wild sea-coast, unprotected by clothes, and without a morsel
of food for twenty-four hours, to procure a coffin for her husband, who had
died by starvation!

CHAPTER: DEATHS IN THE FAMINE (Cork)

By the summer of 1848, Cork was recovering but still devastated. Nicholson
describes "sliding coffins" at the workhouse -- coffins with a hinged bottom
so the body could be dropped into a mass grave and the coffin reused for
the next corpse. She describes a cemetery where ten thousand bodies were
buried in mass graves with lime, calling it "a painful sight" when rain
exposed the remains.

Organizations including the Friends' Society (Quakers), the British
Association, and Father Theobald Mathew distributed American donations.
Abraham Beale, a relief committee member, died of typhus in August 1847
while serving. Nuns cared for approximately 1,300 orphaned children in a
convent near Mathew's chapel.

The chapter concludes by noting the widespread orphaning of children, with
hungry orphans commonly answering they lost their parents "in the stirabout
times" (1847). Terms like "black bread times" and "Soyer's soup" became
lasting cultural references to famine hardship.

CHAPTER: GOING OUT OF IRELAND (Nicholson's departure, 1851)

Nicholson describes packing her trunk and boarding a vessel on the Liffey in
Dublin during darkness, accompanied only by a solitary Quaker. Though friends
had gathered to meet her, the crowd and night prevented meaningful farewells.

She reflects on her satisfaction that "the poor could not find me" for a
final goodbye, noting that their blessing was "a rich reward" for her labor
among them. She expresses her commitment: when her heart stops feeling for
their suffering, may her "tongue cleave to the roof of my mouth."

"I must not enlarge; these things are not mentioned to probe afresh the
painful sensations which philanthropists have felt for Ireland, but to
bear a testimony to facts, which deserve to be recorded; and should any
of these facts appear exaggerated, let it be said that no language is
adequate to give the true, the real picture; one look of the eye into the
daily scenes there witnessed, would overpower what any pen, however
graphic, or tongue, however eloquent, could portray."
