DIARY OF A SOVIET CITIZEN UNDER STALIN
Nina Lugovskaya, "I Want to Live: The Diary of a Young Girl in Stalin's Russia"
Diary entries from 1932-1937
Translated by Andrew Bromfield (2007, Houghton Mifflin)

Nina Lugovskaya (1918-1993) was a Moscow schoolgirl who kept a diary from ages
13 to 18 during the height of Stalin's Terror. Her father, a former Socialist
Revolutionary, had been arrested and exiled multiple times. In 1937, the NKVD
(Soviet secret police) raided the family home and confiscated Nina's diary.
Passages critical of Stalin and the Soviet regime were underlined in red by
NKVD agents and used as evidence to convict the entire family of "anti-Soviet
agitation." Nina and her two sisters were sentenced to five years in a labor
camp in Kolyma, Siberia. Her parents were also imprisoned.

After the fall of the Soviet Union, the diary was discovered in KGB archives
by Irina Osipova, a researcher with the human rights organization Memorial.
In published editions, the passages underlined by the NKVD appear in bold type.

Nina has been called "the Anne Frank of Russia." Unlike Anne Frank, she survived
the camps and lived until 1993, becoming a painter and theatre designer. She
never spoke publicly about her diary.

SELECTED DIARY EXCERPTS

October 1932 (age 13):

"Today they herded us out to march around the streets, which made me absolutely
furious.... Walking over the cold, gray ground in the damp, dull light of an
autumn day... and cursing Soviet power to myself."

[This passage was underlined by the NKVD.]

---

On life under the Soviet system:

"To hell with the new society! It's only Gennady who can get wrapped up in it
and spend hours reading what Lenin and Stalin said and about the achievements
of our Soviet Union."

[Underlined by the NKVD.]

---

On a public celebration:

"I heard the orchestra playing the march and, somewhere in the distance,
cries of 'Hurrah!'"

---

On the economic situation:

"A kilo of white bread costs 60 kopeks! A litre of kerosene costs 50!"

"Angry, hungry, tired people standing in lines berate the government and
curse life."

---

On political opposition:

"I felt glad: that means there's still a struggle going on, there are still
organisations..."

[Underlined by the NKVD.]

---

On her private feelings:

"My thoughts are full of boys and compared to them everything else seems
uninteresting..."

"I'm just dragging out a pitiful existence."

---

On school:

"We're behaving abominably in class. Our fear of the teachers has
disappeared..."

---

On Ukraine during the Holodomor:

"Ukraine... what has happened to it? It's unrecognisable. Nothing but the
lifeless, silent steppe..."

---

On Stalin:

"I decided that I had to kill the bastards."

[This passage, referring to Stalin, was heavily underlined by the NKVD and
became a key piece of evidence in the family's conviction.]

---

On adaptation and survival:

"I'm almost satisfied with my life. I had to adapt and with every year
this ability..."

---

Final diary entry, January 2, 1937:

"I'm looking ahead and only ahead."

[Five months later, on June 4, 1937, NKVD agents came for the family.
Nina was 18 years old.]

CONTEXT NOTE FOR EDUCATORS:

Nina's diary is remarkable because it shows an ordinary teenager's life
intersecting with state terror. Her entries alternate between the universal
concerns of adolescence -- crushes, friendships, homework, self-doubt --
and the specific horrors of Stalinist Russia: her father's repeated arrests,
the bread lines, the enforced marches, the atmosphere of fear and surveillance.

The diary demonstrates how totalitarian regimes reach into private life.
The very act of writing honestly in a diary became a criminal offense. The
NKVD's red underlines -- visible in the published edition -- show exactly
which private thoughts the state considered dangerous.
