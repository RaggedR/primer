PRIMO LEVI, "IF THIS IS A MAN" (1947)
Also published in English as "Survival in Auschwitz"
Translated from the Italian by Stuart Woolf

Primo Levi (1919-1987) was an Italian Jewish chemist from Turin who was
arrested as a member of the anti-Fascist resistance in December 1943 and
deported to the Buna-Monowitz labour camp (Auschwitz III) in February 1944.
He survived ten months in the camp, partly because his chemistry training
made him useful in a rubber factory. He was liberated by Soviet troops on
27 January 1945. "If This Is a Man" was written in 1946-47 and first
published in Italian as "Se questo e un uomo" in 1947. It is widely
regarded as one of the most important testimonies of the twentieth century.

The excerpts below follow the Stuart Woolf translation (1959), which Levi
supervised. The Feldman/Swann translation of the poem is also noted below.

==========================================================================
EPIGRAPH: "SHEMA" (10 January 1946)
==========================================================================

[Stuart Woolf translation, as it appears in the book]

You who live safe
In your warm houses,
You who find, returning in the evening,
Hot food and friendly faces:

Consider if this is a man
Who works in the mud
Who does not know peace
Who fights for a scrap of bread
Who dies because of a yes or a no.
Consider if this is a woman,
Without hair and without name
With no more strength to remember,
Her eyes empty and her womb cold
Like a frog in winter.

Meditate that this came about:
I commend these words to you.
Carve them in your hearts
At home, in the street,
Going to bed, rising;
Repeat them to your children.
Or may your house fall apart,
May illness impede you,
May your children turn their faces from you.

[Ruth Feldman and Brian Swann translation, from "Shema: Collected Poems"]

You who live secure
In your warm houses,
Who return at evening to find
Hot food and friendly faces:

Consider whether this is a man,
Who labours in the mud
Who knows no peace
Who fights for a crust of bread
Who dies at a yes or a no.
Consider whether this is a woman,
Without hair or name
With no more strength to remember
Eyes empty and womb cold
As a frog in winter.

Consider that this has been:
I commend these words to you.
Engrave them on your hearts
When you are in your house, when you walk on your way,
When you go to bed, when you rise.
Repeat them to your children.
Or may your house crumble,
Disease render you powerless,
Your offspring avert their faces from you.

[Note: The title "Shema" comes from the Hebrew prayer "Shema Yisrael"
("Hear, O Israel"), Deuteronomy 6:4-9. Levi's closing injunction --
"Engrave them on your hearts / When you are in your house, when you walk
on your way, / When you go to bed, when you rise. / Repeat them to your
children" -- directly echoes the Deuteronomy text. But where the Torah
commands the faithful to engrave God's words, Levi commands the reader
to engrave the memory of what was done to human beings. The final lines
are not a prayer but a curse: may your house crumble if you forget.]

FROM CHAPTER 2: "ON THE BOTTOM"
==========================================================================

[Arrival at Buna-Monowitz. The prisoners have been transported in sealed
cattle wagons, separated from women and children on the platform at
Auschwitz, stripped, shaved, showered, and given camp uniforms.]

This is hell. Today, in our times, hell must be like this. A huge, empty
room: we are tired, standing on our feet, with a tap which drips while we
cannot drink the water, and we wait for something which will certainly be
terrible, and nothing happens and nothing continues to happen. What can
one think about? One cannot think any more, it is like being already dead.
Someone sits down on the ground. The time passes drop by drop.

---

Then for the first time we became aware that our language lacks words to
express this offence, the demolition of a man. In a moment, with almost
prophetic intuition, the reality was revealed to us: we had reached the
bottom. It is not possible to sink lower than this; no human condition is
more miserable than this, nor could it conceivably be so. Nothing belongs
to us any more; they have taken away our clothes, our shoes, even our
hair; if we speak, they will not listen to us, and if they listen, they
will not understand. They will even take away our name: and if we want to
keep it, we will have to find ourselves the strength to do so, to manage
somehow so that behind the name something of us, of us as we were, still
remains.

---

Haftling: I have learned that I am a Haftling. My name is 174517; we
have been baptised, we will carry the tattoo on our left arm until we die.

---

Imagine now a man who is deprived of everyone he loves, and at the same
time of his house, his habits, his clothes, in short, of everything he
possesses: he will be a hollow man, reduced to suffering and needs,
forgetful of dignity and restraint, for he who loses all often easily
loses himself. He will be a man whose life or death can be lightly decided
with no sense of human affinity, in the most fortunate of cases, on the
basis of a pure judgment of utility. It is in this way that one can
understand the double sense of the term "extermination camp," and it is
now clear what we seek to express with the phrase: "to lie on the bottom."

---

[The episode of "Hier ist kein Warum" -- "Here there is no why"]

Driven by thirst, I eyed a fine icicle outside the window, within hand's
reach. I opened the window and broke off the icicle but at once a large,
heavy guard prowling outside brutally snatched it away from me. "Warum?"
I asked him in my poor German. "Hier ist kein warum" (here there is no
why), he replied, pushing me inside with a shove.

FROM CHAPTER 9: "THE DROWNED AND THE SAVED"
==========================================================================

[Note: This chapter in "If This Is a Man" should not be confused with
Levi's later book of the same title, published in 1986. The chapter
lays out the moral taxonomy of the camp: those who survived ("the saved")
and those who perished ("the drowned"). Levi's analysis insists that
survival was rarely a matter of moral superiority.]

We do not believe in the most obvious and facile deduction: that man is
fundamentally brutal, egoistic and stupid in his conduct once every
civilised institution is taken away, and that the Haftling is consequently
nothing but a man without inhibitions. We believe, rather, that the only
conclusion to be drawn is that in the face of driving necessity and
physical disabilities many social habits and instincts are reduced to
silence.

---

[The Muselmann -- the "drowned"]

Their life is short, but their number is endless; they, the Muselmanner,
the drowned, form the backbone of the camp, an anonymous mass, continually
renewed and always identical, of non-men who march and labour in silence,
the divine spark dead within them, already too empty to really suffer.
One hesitates to call them living: one hesitates to call their death
death, in the face of which they have no fear, as they are too tired to
understand.

They crowd my memory with their faceless presences, and if I could enclose
all the evil of our time in one image, I would choose this image which is
familiar to me: an emaciated man, with head drooped and shoulders curved,
on whose face and in whose eyes not a trace of a thought is to be seen.

---

[On survival and the "saved"]

To survive without renouncing any part of one's own moral world -- apart
from powerful and direct interventions by fortune -- was conceded only to
very few superior individuals, made of the stuff of martyrs and saints.

---

[On the camp's logic: to those who have, more shall be given]

The easiest thing is to succumb: one has only to carry out all the orders
one receives, to eat only the ration, to observe the discipline of the
work and the camp. Experience showed that only exceptionally could one
survive more than three months in this way.

FROM CHAPTER 11: "THE CANTO OF ULYSSES"
==========================================================================

[Levi, walking with his French companion Jean, tries to remember Canto
XXVI of Dante's Inferno -- Ulysses' speech to his crew -- and translate
it into French on the spot. The passage is one of the most celebrated
in the book: Dante's poetry about the nature of humanity becomes a
lifeline in the camp.]

"Consider your origin: you were not made to live as brutes, but to
pursue virtue and knowledge."

As if I also was hearing it for the first time: like the blast of a
trumpet, like the voice of God. For a moment I forget who I am and
where I am.

==========================================================================
FROM CHAPTER 17: "THE STORY OF TEN DAYS"
==========================================================================

[After the SS evacuated the camp on 18 January 1945, forcing most
prisoners on the death marches, Levi -- ill with scarlet fever in the
camp infirmary -- was among those left behind.]

The first Russian patrol came in sight of the camp around midday on
27 January 1945.

---

It was the shame we knew so well, the shame that drowned us after the
selections, and every time we had to watch, or submit to, some outrage:
the shame that the Germans did not know, that the just man experiences
at another man's crime; the feeling of guilt that such a crime should
exist, that it should have been introduced irrevocably into the world
of things that exist, and that his will for good should have proved too
weak or null, and should not have availed in defence.

FROM THE APPENDIX (1976 edition) AND LATER ESSAYS
==========================================================================

[On the duty of testimony]

We must be listened to: above and beyond our personal experience, we have
collectively witnessed a fundamental unexpected event, fundamental
precisely because unexpected, not foreseen by anyone. It happened,
therefore it can happen again: this is the core of what we have to say.

---

[On reason vs. charisma -- from "The Drowned and the Saved," 1986]

It is, therefore, necessary to be suspicious of those who seek to
convince us with means other than reason, and of charismatic leaders:
we must be cautious about delegating to others our judgement and our will.

---

[On civilisation as protection of the weak]

A country is considered the more civilised the more the wisdom and
efficiency of its laws hinder a weak man from becoming too weak and a
powerful one too powerful.

---

[On the refusal of consent]

...this was the sense, not forgotten either then or later: that precisely
because the Lager was a great machine to reduce us to beasts, we must not
become beasts; that even in this place one can survive, and therefore one
must want to survive, to tell the story, to bear witness; and that to
survive we must force ourselves to save at least the skeleton, the
scaffolding, the form of civilisation. We are slaves, deprived of every
right, exposed to every insult, condemned to certain death, but we still
possess one power, and we must defend it with all our strength for it is
the last -- the power to refuse our consent.

---

To destroy a man is difficult, almost as difficult as to create one: it
was not easy, it was not quick, but you Germans have succeeded. Here we
are, docile under your gaze; from our side you have nothing more to fear;
no acts of violence, no words of defiance, not even a look of judgement.
