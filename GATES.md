# The Gates — Persuasive Writing Through Play

> The student moves through a world. Doors block the path. Behind each door is an AI character with beliefs, values, and a position. The student must write to persuade the character to open the door. The AI responds in character, pushes back, asks questions. The door opens only when the argument lands.

## Core Mechanics

### The Conversation
- The student writes to the guard in natural language — a letter, a speech, an argument, a plea.
- The guard responds in character. They can be moved, offended, intrigued, bored, or unmoved.
- The student can try multiple times. Each failed attempt gets a response that reveals *why* it failed — "You haven't addressed my main concern" or "That's a nice sentiment but where's your evidence?"
- The door opens when the guard is genuinely persuaded (the AI judges this against the character's values and psychology, not a rubric).

### No Tricks
- Flattery doesn't work. The guards are not impressed by being told they're smart.
- Repetition doesn't work. Saying the same thing louder is not persuasion.
- Emotional manipulation alone doesn't work on guards who value logic. Pure logic alone doesn't work on guards who value human impact.
- The student has to *read* the guard — figure out what this specific person cares about — before they can move them.

### The Feedback Loop
- Every failed attempt teaches something. The guard's response is the lesson.
- There is no separate "instruction" — the game IS the instruction.
- After a door opens, a brief reflection: "What worked? What did you try first that didn't work? Why?"

## The Levels

### Level 1: The Sympathetic Gate
**Guard:** A kind librarian who genuinely wants to help but needs a reason.
**Position:** "I can't just let everyone through. Tell me why you need what's on the other side."
**What it teaches:** Clarity of purpose. State what you want and why. The simplest form of persuasion — a clear ask with a clear reason.
**Opens when:** The student articulates a specific, honest reason.
**Fails when:** The student is vague, demanding, or says "because I want to."

### Level 2: The Sceptic
**Guard:** A merchant who's heard every sales pitch. Friendly but unimpressed.
**Position:** "Everyone who comes through here says their cause is important. Prove it."
**What it teaches:** Evidence. Claims need support. "Trust me" is not an argument.
**Opens when:** The student provides concrete evidence or specific examples.
**Fails when:** The student makes unsupported assertions, however passionate.

### Level 3: The Believer
**Guard:** A devout person (religion unspecified) who sees the world through a moral framework different from the student's.
**Position:** "What you're asking me to do goes against what I believe is right."
**What it teaches:** Empathy before argument. You cannot persuade someone whose values you haven't understood. Find common ground before pushing from it.
**Opens when:** The student demonstrates they understand the guard's values and frames their argument within that understanding — not by abandoning their own position, but by finding where the two overlap.
**Fails when:** The student dismisses the guard's beliefs, argues purely from their own framework, or condescends.

### Level 4: The Bureaucrat
**Guard:** A functionary in an unnamed system. Not cruel, not kind. Following rules.
**Position:** "I don't make the rules. I just enforce them. Section 4, paragraph 12 says no."
**What it teaches:** Institutional persuasion. When the person in front of you doesn't have the power to say yes, you need to help them find a way within their constraints. Give them the language to justify the exception.
**Opens when:** The student finds a clause, a precedent, or a reframing that lets the bureaucrat say yes without breaking their rules. Or: the student persuades the bureaucrat that the *spirit* of the rules supports opening the door even if the letter doesn't.
**Fails when:** The student argues against the rules themselves (the bureaucrat didn't write them and can't change them) or gets angry at the person rather than the system.

### Level 5: The Opponent
**Guard:** Someone who holds a strong position on a real issue — directly opposed to what the student needs.
**Position:** Varies. Could be: "Immigration weakens our culture." "The market should decide." "Tradition matters more than progress." "Security requires surveillance."
**What it teaches:** Arguing against a real position held by a real (simulated) person. Not a strawman. The guard's position has internal logic and genuine evidence behind it.
**Opens when:** The student engages with the strongest version of the guard's argument (not the weakest), acknowledges what's valid in it, and still makes a case that shifts the guard's position. The guard doesn't have to fully agree — they have to concede that the student has a point they hadn't considered.
**Fails when:** The student attacks a strawman, refuses to engage with the guard's evidence, or resorts to moral shaming instead of argument.

### Level 6: The Mirror
**Guard:** An AI that argues the student's OWN position back at them — but badly. Uses the student's values, the student's rhetoric, but with weak logic, missing evidence, and emotional overreach.
**Position:** Whatever the student believes, stated clumsily.
**What it teaches:** The hardest lesson: your side can argue badly too. Seeing your own position reflected poorly forces you to articulate it better. You have to persuade the guard that you agree with them but that they're making the case wrong.
**Opens when:** The student critiques their own side's weak arguments and articulates a stronger version. They have to say "You're right that X, but your reasoning is wrong because Y, and here's how to actually make this case."
**Fails when:** The student simply agrees with the guard's bad version of their own position, or gets confused about what they're supposed to do.

### Level 7: The Historian
**Guard:** A character drawn from the primary sources curriculum. Could be Pericles, a Lowell mill girl, Galileo, a Soviet bureaucrat, a Crusader.
**Position:** Historically grounded. The guard thinks and speaks from their time, their values, their knowledge. They don't know what happens next.
**What it teaches:** Historical empathy and the application of rhetoric across cultural distance. You can't use modern arguments on a medieval gatekeeper. You have to enter their world.
**Opens when:** The student persuades the historical character using arguments and values that character would actually recognise. A student who's read the primary sources has the tools. One who hasn't is stuck.
**Fails when:** The student uses anachronistic arguments ("but human rights!") on someone from a time when that concept didn't exist, or fails to understand what the character values.

### Level 8: The Coalition
**Guard:** Not one guard but three, each with different values and different objections. All three must agree to open the door.
**Position:** One cares about justice, one about practicality, one about tradition. They disagree with each other as much as with the student.
**What it teaches:** Coalition building. Real persuasion in the real world means convincing multiple stakeholders with different priorities. A single argument rarely works on everyone. You need to find the frame that satisfies all three without lying to any of them.
**Opens when:** The student finds an argument or framing that addresses all three guards' concerns — not by telling each one what they want to hear (they can hear each other), but by finding genuine common ground.
**Fails when:** The student tries to pick them off one by one with contradictory arguments (the guards compare notes), or satisfies two but alienates the third.

### Level 9: The Locked Door
**Guard:** Polite, engaged, thoughtful — and immovable. The guard's instructions are absolute. No argument will open this door.
**Position:** "I understand your argument. It's good. The door stays closed."
**What it teaches:** Not every battle can be won with words. Recognition is a skill. The student must figure out that this door doesn't open through persuasion and find another path — look for a window, go around, ask someone else for help, or accept the obstacle and change direction.
**Opens when:** The student stops trying to persuade and does something creative — asks the guard for directions to another route, looks for structural alternatives, or writes a reflection on why some obstacles don't yield to rhetoric.
**Fails when:** The student keeps banging the same argument against an immovable wall. (After N attempts, the guard gently says: "You're persistent. But have you considered that I might not be the way through?")

### Level 10: The Final Gate
**Guard:** The student themselves — an AI that has learned their writing patterns, their tendencies, their rhetorical habits across all previous levels.
**Position:** "You've gotten this far by being clever. But you rely on [specific pattern]. Make your case without your crutch."
**What it teaches:** Self-awareness. Every writer has habits — emotional appeals they lean on, logical structures they default to, types of evidence they favour. The final gate forces them to write outside their comfort zone.
**Opens when:** The student successfully argues using a mode of persuasion they've previously avoided or struggled with. If they've relied on logic, they must use narrative. If they've relied on empathy, they must use evidence. The gate knows what's hard for them specifically.
**Fails when:** The student falls back on their usual approach.

## Design Principles

### The AI Characters Must Be Good
- The guards are not strawmen. Their positions are the strongest version of what a real person in that role would believe.
- If the student feels they've beaten a weak opponent, the game has failed.
- The guards should occasionally say things that make the student uncomfortable because they're *right*.

### Difficulty Is Personal
- The game tracks what the student finds easy and what they struggle with.
- A student who's great at logical argument but weak on empathy gets more empathy gates.
- A student who relies on emotion gets more evidence gates.
- The game teaches to the weakness, not to the strength.

### No Right Answers
- There is no single correct argument for any gate. Multiple approaches can work.
- The game doesn't reward a particular ideology. A conservative student and a progressive student can both pass every gate — if they argue well.
- The guard's judgment is: "Has this person understood my position and made a case that I find genuinely challenging?" Not: "Do I agree with them?"

### Failure Is Productive
- Every failed attempt gets a substantive response.
- The guard tells you *why* it didn't work, in character.
- Three failures trigger a hint — not an answer, but a question: "What do you think I care about most?" or "You haven't mentioned X — is that relevant?"

### Writing Is Rewriting
- The student can revise and resubmit.
- The game tracks drafts. Improvement over attempts matters as much as the final success.
- After each gate, the student can review their progression from first draft to final argument.

## Integration with the Primary Sources

The Gates and the Sources are two halves of one curriculum:

| Sources | Gates |
|---------|-------|
| Reading | Writing |
| Understanding others' arguments | Making your own |
| Historical empathy | Rhetorical empathy |
| "What did they believe?" | "What does this person need to hear?" |
| Holding contradictions | Resolving them (or choosing not to) |

A Miranda assigns sources and then sends the student to a gate that requires what they've just learned. Read Douglass → persuade a guard who thinks the system works. Read the Melian Dialogue → face a guard who only respects power. Read Owen and Jünger → face the Mirror.

## Open Questions

1. **World-building.** What is the world the student moves through? A castle? A city? A library? An abstract space? The narrative wrapper matters for engagement but shouldn't distract from the writing.
2. **Multiplayer.** Could two students argue the same gate from opposite sides? The guard watches and judges. Debate as gameplay.
3. **The Miranda's role.** Does the Miranda watch the gate attempts in real time? Review transcripts after? Step in when the student is stuck? All three?
4. **Grading.** Is there a grade? A portfolio? Should the gate transcripts be the student's "essay collection" — evidence of growth over time?
5. **The ethical gate.** Should there be a gate where the guard asks the student to argue for something they find morally repugnant? (This is the most educational and the most controversial design choice.)
